# Comparative Evaluation of Convolutional and Transformer-Based Detectors for Automated Weed Detection in Precision Agriculture 

Alcides Toledo Espinosa 1 , Gerardo Antonio Alvarez Hernandez 1 , Ángel Eduardo Zamora-Suárez 2 , Miguel Bolaños 1 , and Juan Irving Vasquez 1

Abstract. This paper presents a comparative evaluation of convolu-tional and transformer-based object detection architectures for early weed detection in realistic scenarios. Representative models from each paradigm are considered, including YOLOv26-nano, a recent variant of the YOLO family, and transformer-based approaches such as RT-DETR and RF-DETR. Experiments were conducted on the GROUND-BASED_WEED dataset, allowing performance to be evaluated in terms of detection accuracy and computational efficiency using metrics such as precision, recall, average precision, and inference speed. The results highlight a clear trade-off between efficiency and contex-tual modeling: CNN-based detectors achieve high performance at a lower computational cost, while transformer-based approaches offer better global context capture at the expense of higher resource demands. These results provide practical criteria for model selection in precision agriculture ap-plications. 

Keywords: Smart agriculture · Weed detection · YOLOv26 · RT-DETR · Vision transformers 

## 1 Introduction 

Agricultural systems are currently undergoing a profound transformation driven by the dual need to increase productivity and ensure environmental sustainabil-ity. In this context, the development of intelligent systems capable of perceiving, interpreting, and acting in complex and dynamic agricultural environments has become a fundamental requirement. Among the various challenges faced in mod-ern agriculture, accurate weed identification and management are crucial because they directly impact crop yield, resource use efficiency, and ecological balance. Consequently, the integration of advanced computer vision and deep learning techniques for cropweed discrimination has emerged as a central research topic in precision agriculture. Precision agriculture is founded on the use of advanced technologies to opti-mize crop production while minimizing environmental impact [1,2]. This paradigm relies on site-specific management strategies that exploit the spatial and tempo-ral variability of agricultural fields to improve decision-making. In recent years,

In particular, deep learning ap-proaches have demonstrated remarkable capabilities for extracting meaningful representations from high-dimensional data, thereby improving performance in tasks such as classification, detection, and decision-making [3]. Modern sensing platforms, including unmanned aerial vehicles and ground-based robotic sys-tems, provide high-resolution and dynamic perception capabilities, facilitating continuous monitoring and automated intervention. Within this framework, weed detection constitutes a critical application due to its direct implications for both productivity and environmental sustainabil-ity [4]. Accurate weed identification enables the implementation of site-specific interventions, such as precision spraying, which significantly reduces excessive herbicide use and mitigates environmental damage [5]. However, despite these advances, achieving robust and generalizable solutions remains challenging. Real agricultural environments exhibit high variability in illumination conditions, plant morphology, soil backgrounds, and occlusions, which complicates the de-velopment of reliable visual recognition systems. Weeds are among the most significant biotic stressors affecting agricultural productivity. They compete with crops for essential resources such as nutrients, water, and sunlight, leading to considerable yield losses if not properly controlled [5]. Furthermore, weeds can serve as hosts for pests and diseases, exacerbating their negative impact on crop health. Traditional weed management strategies, such as mechanical removal and uniform herbicide application, are often inef-ficient and environmentally unsustainable because they lack spatial selectivity. These approaches result in excessive agrochemical use, increased operational costs, and the emergence of herbicide-resistant species, underscoring the need for more precise and sustainable solutions. A particularly challenging scenario arises during the early growth stages of crops, which correspond to the critical intervention period. During this phase, crops and weeds exhibit high morphological similarity, making visual discrimina-tion difficult even for experienced agronomists. From a computational perspec-tive, this problem can be formulated as a fine-grained visual classification task, characterized by high intra-class variability and low inter-class separability. Ad-ditionally, agricultural environments are inherently unstructured and dynamic, with significant variations in lighting conditions, soil textures, plant density, and occlusions. These factors introduce noise and uncertainty, limiting the ro-bustness and generalization capabilities of existing computer vision models [3]. Consequently, the development of reliable and efficient methods for early-stage weed detection remains an open and challenging research problem. Deep learning has substantially advanced the state of the art in computer vision for agricultural applications. In particular, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have demonstrated strong performance across a wide range of appli-cations, including image classification, object detection, and semantic segmen-tation, consistently outperforming traditional approaches based on handcrafted Comparative Evaluation of CNN and Transformer Detectors 3

features [6,7]. These models are particularly effective at capturing local spatial patterns, such as edges, textures, and shapes. However, in fine-grained classi-fication scenarios, such as early-stage cropweed discrimination, CNN-based ar-chitectures may be limited by their inherently localized receptive fields, which restrict their ability to capture long-range dependencies and global contextual information. To overcome these limitations, transformer-based architectures have emerged as a promising alternative. Vision Transformers (ViTs) model images as se-quences of patches and employ self-attention mechanisms to capture global re-lationships across the entire image [8]. Similarly, transformer-based detection frameworks such as DETR provide end-to-end object detection solutions without requiring region proposal mechanisms [9]. Furthermore, recent advances in self-supervised learning have enhanced transformer models’ ability to learn robust and transferable representations [10]. Despite these advantages, transformer-based models typically require large-scale datasets and significant computational resources, which may limit their applicability in real-world agricultural scenarios. Therefore, hybrid approaches that combine CNN-based local feature extraction with transformer-based global reasoning have been proposed. Despite the significant progress achieved in recent years, several limitations remain. Many studies have been conducted under controlled or semi-controlled conditions that do not reflect the complexity of real-world agricultural environ-ments, leading to models with limited robustness and generalization. In partic-ular, early-stage cropweed discrimination remains insufficiently explored. Addi-tionally, the limited availability of diverse, well-annotated datasets introduces domain-shift challenges. While CNN-based methods have been extensively stud-ied, their limitations in capturing global context persist; conversely, although transformer-based approaches are promising, they have not been thoroughly evaluated under real-world conditions. Moreover, there is a lack of systematic and fair comparisons between convolutional and transformer-based architectures within a unified experimental framework. Motivated by these challenges, this work presents a comprehensive evalua-tion of deep learning models for crop-weed classification under realistic condi-tions, with particular emphasis on early-stage detection. Using a high-resolution dataset, this study systematically analyzes trade-offs among robustness, gen-eralization, and computational efficiency across model families, including con-volutional, transformer-based, and hybrid architectures. This analysis aims to contribute to the development of reliable perception systems for precision agri-culture in real-world environments. ## 2 Materials and Methods 

This section presents the methodological framework for evaluating convolutional and transformer-based object detection architectures for early-stage weed detec-tion under real-field conditions. Emphasis is placed on realistic datasets, stan-dardized protocols, and reproducible implementation. The methodology is organized into five components: (2.1) dataset descrip-tion, (2.2) convolution-based detectors, (2.3) transformer-based detectors, (2.4) experimental design, and (2.5) implementation. This structure enables a fair and systematic comparison of different deep learning approaches under identical conditions. Model robustness and generalization are evaluated under real-world condi-tions, including class imbalance, annotation noise, and high morphological sim-ilarity between crops and weeds. 

2.1 Dataset 

The public GROUNDBASED_WEED dataset was used for evaluation. It con-sists of high-resolution RGB images ( 5184 × 3888 pixels) captured in commer-cial tomato fields in Santa Amalia, Spain, using a Canon PowerShot SX540 HS camera [11]. The dataset reflects realistic agricultural conditions, incorporating natural variability in illumination and scene composition. Images were acquired from a birds-eye perspective at a height of 11.5 m, yielding a ground sampling distance of approximately 0.05 cm/pixel. 2.2 Convolution-Based Detectors 

Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)-based object detectors capture a dataset’s original complexity by training models to achieve strong performance and com-putational efficiency. These methods are typically categorized into two-stage and one-stage detectors, offering different trade-offs between accuracy and speed. Two-stage detectors, such as the R-CNN family [16,17], achieve high accu-racy thresholds, but their sequential processing limits real-time applicability. In contrast, one-stage detectors directly predict bounding boxes and class probabil-ities in a single forward pass, enabling faster inference. Among these, the YOLO family [18] has become a dominant approach for real-time object detection. Recent advancements, such as YOLOv26-nano [19], further improve efficiency and detection performance by simplifying the detection pipeline and optimizing feature representation, making them suitable for real-time applications. CNN-based detectors are particularly effective at capturing local spatial fea-tures, such as edges, textures, and shapes, which are essential for object discrim-ination. However, their limited ability to model global contextual relationships may reduce performance in fine-grained scenarios, such as early-stage cropweed detection. 

2.3 Transformer-Based Detectors 

Transformer-based architectures have introduced a new paradigm in object de-tection by leveraging self-attention mechanisms to capture global contextual relationships within images. This approach was introduced by the Detection Transformer (DETR) [9], which formulates detection as a direct set prediction problem within an end-to-end framework. However, the original DETR suffers from slow convergence and limited per-formance on small objects due to insufficient multi-scale feature representation. To address these limitations, several improved variants have been proposed. Among them, RT-DETR [20] introduces a hybrid architecture that combines convolutional backbones with transformer-based attention modules, enabling a better balance between accuracy and real-time performance. As a result, it has become one of the first transformer-based detectors to compete with state-of-the-art CNN-based models in both accuracy and latency. Transformer-based detectors are particularly effective in modeling long-range dependencies and global context, which is critical in fine-grained tasks such as early-stage cropweed detection. However, they typically require higher compu-tational resources and larger datasets, limiting their applicability in real-time or resource-constrained environments. Overall, these models provide improved contextual modeling while highlight-ing trade-offs between accuracy and computational efficiency. 

2.4 Design of Experiment 

This study presents a comparative evaluation of object detection architectures based on convolutional networks and transformers for early weed detection under Comparative Evaluation of CNN and Transformer Detectors 7

real-world field conditions. The YOLOv26-nano model was selected as a repre-sentative of CNN-based detectors due to its lightweight design, which is par-ticularly relevant for agricultural applications where deployment on embedded systems is sought. Similarly, lightweight variants of transformer-based architec-tures, including RT-DETR Medium and RT-DETR Large, were selected to en-sure a fair comparison under practical computational constraints. The objective is to evaluate detection accuracy, generalization, and computational efficiency in challenging agricultural environments. All models were trained under a standardized protocol to ensure fair compar-ison. Training was performed for up to 200 epochs with early stopping (patience = 20). The AdamW optimizer was used with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5, and a batch size of 4 was selected to balance memory constraints and training stability. The full complexity of the GROUNDBASED_WEED dataset was preserved by retaining all 7 annotated classes, enabling rigorous evaluation in a fine-grained classification setting. To reduce computational cost, a multi-scale resizing strategy was applied, evaluating input resolutions of 640, 1080, and 2160 pixels. This enables the analysis of the trade-off between detection accuracy and computational efficiency. The dataset was partitioned into training, validation, and test sets with an 80%10%10% split, yielding 972 images for training and 122 images each for validation and testing. This partitioning strategy ensures that model training, hyperparameter tuning, and performance evaluation are conducted on indepen-dent subsets, supporting an unbiased assessment of model performance. Performance was assessed using standard object detection metrics, including Precision, Recall, and mean Average Precision (mAP@50 and mAP@50–95), in accordance with the COCO evaluation protocol. These metrics are consistent with those reported by Adrià et al. (2025) [21], enabling a direct and fair com-parison on the same dataset. An Intersection over Union (IoU) threshold of 0.5 and a confidence threshold of 0.3 were adopted during evaluation. Inference efficiency was also assessed through latency and frames per second (FPS), providing insight into the suitability of each model for real-time applica-tions. This experimental setup enables a consistent comparison between CNN-and transformer-based detectors, with emphasis on deployment efficiency. 

2.5 Implementation 

All experiments were conducted in a reproducible environment using Python 3.10 with Conda for dependency management. Models were implemented in Py-Torch with GPU acceleration. The Ultralytics library was used for CNN-based detectors (YOLOv26), while transformer-based models (RT-DETR) were imple-mented using their official PyTorch frameworks. Experiments were performed on a workstation with an Intel Core i7 processor, 32 GB RAM, and an NVIDIA RTX 2080 Super GPU (8 GB), with CUDA and cuDNN enabled for acceleration. Reproducibility was ensured through fixed random seeds, identical data splits, and consistent preprocessing pipelines. Model checkpoints were selected based on 8 A. Toledo et al. 

validation performance, and mixed precision (FP16) was used when supported to reduce memory usage and accelerate training. Training times ranged from 6 to 8 hours per model, with transformer-based models requiring higher computational resources, while CNN-based models showed faster convergence and lower overhead. Inference performance was evaluated in terms of latency and frames per sec-ond (FPS), highlighting the trade-off between representational capacity and real-time applicability. 

## 3 Results and Discussion 

To evaluate the performance of the selected detection frameworks, a series of experiments was conducted using the hyperparameters specified in Section 2.4. The evaluated models YOLOv26-nano, RT-DETR, and RF-DETR were cho-sen as representative state-of-the-art approaches. Specifically, this selection con-trasts a highly optimized convolution-based model (YOLOv26-nano) with two transformer-based architectures (RT-DETR and RF-DETR) to analyze the trade-offs between detection accuracy and computational overhead. As summarized in Table 1, YOLOv26-N and RT-DETR exhibit compara-ble performance regarding Precision, Recall, and mAP @50 , especially at higher resolutions. The robustness of YOLOv26-nano stems from its efficient single-stage design, which leverages hierarchical spatial feature extraction to detect multi-scale objects at a low computational cost. Conversely, RT-DETR inte-grates self-attention mechanisms to model global context, potentially enhancing object localization in complex scenes.

Furthermore, it is crucial to note that the slight performance gains in RT-DETR entail a considerable increase in computational complexity. Due to its Transformer-based backbone, RT-DETR requires higher memory allocation and yields longer inference times, which may hinder its feasibility for real-time de-ployment on embedded robotic platforms. In the case of RF-DETR , the model followed a similar trend but consistently underperformed across all metrics, po-tentially due to the sensitivity of attention-based architectures to dataset scale and hyperparameter tuning. It is essential to contextualize these findings with respect to prior studies, such as the work of [21], which used the same dataset and reported higher perfor-mance metrics. However, it is important to note that those results were obtained under a simplified classification setting involving only five classes, whereas the present study considers a more comprehensive scheme with seven classes. By preserving the complete set of labels, this work introduces a more challenging and realistic evaluation scenario. Lastly, increasing the input resolution from 640 to 2160 pixels yielded only negligible improvements. This suggests that the challenge lies in the dataset’s intrinsic characteristics: since the target objects occupy a very small fraction of the image, higher resolutions do not necessarily yield more discriminative fea-tures. Overall, the results indicate that YOLOv26 nano offers the most favorable balance between accuracy and efficiency, making it the most suitable candidate for real-world precision agriculture scenarios. Given that the YOLOv26-nano model demonstrated the most balanced per-formance, qualitative inferences were conducted to visually validate the detec-tion robustness, with examples presented in Figure 2. It is worth noting that the overall metrics reflect the dataset’s inherent imbalance. Although a stratified partitioning strategy was implemented to mitigate class imbalance, a disparity in inter-class performance persists. Furthermore, minor labeling omissions were identified within the original image bank; however, to preserve the integrity and reproducibility of the benchmark dataset, manual re-labeling was avoided. Con-sequently, these limitations were accepted as part of the model’s generalization challenges under real-world data constraints. 

## 4 Conclusions 

This study presented a comparative evaluation of the two dominant paradigms in modern object detection: convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and attention-based mechanisms (Transformers), specifically applied to the critical challenge of weed identification. The results demonstrate that, given the irregular morphol-ogy and the large-scale variability of plant species, multi-scale detection remains the determining factor for system success. Although Transformer-based models offer superior global context modeling, the YOLOv26-nano model achieved com-parable performance while offering significantly higher computational efficiency. 10 A. It is concluded that hierarchical CNN optimization provides a more robust ap-proach for real-time precision agriculture applications, particularly for handling small, dispersed objects. Consequently, YOLOv26-nano emerges as the most vi-able architecture for integration into embedded robotic systems operating under real-world field conditions. Building on these findings, future research will explore instance segmentation architectures to achieve a more precise delineation of irregular weed morphologies than traditional bounding boxes. Additionally, we aim to address the inherent dataset imbalance by employing synthetic data augmentation techniques, such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) or diffusion models, to enhance the representation of minority classes.
