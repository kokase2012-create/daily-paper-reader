# Abstract 

Image-based crop and pest recognition is considered useful for reducing the delay and cost of manual field scouting, therefore supporting timely intervention in precision-agriculture workflows. However, the real field imagery remains challenging due to the cluttered backgrounds, occlusions, illumination changes, and strong scale variation that are frequently observed across crops. The symptoms are often small or low-contrast, and pests may be partially hidden, which reduces the reliability when the setting is outside controlled environments. A unified multi-class crop–pest/condition recognition framework is presented, where a ResNet-50 backbone is utilized and enhanced with a Multi-Scale Contextual Attention (MSCA) module. The novelty is mainly considered to be achieved through the integration of explicit multi-scale contextual aggregation with lightweight joint channel and spatial attention by means of residual fusion, while the empirical evaluation was kept controlled under a fixed and reproducible protocol. A curated dataset of 21,404 field-style images covering 15 crop and pest/condition classes was compiled, and a leakage-aware fixed split with a held-out test set was adopted to support reproducibility. Augmentation was applied only to the training subset to improve robustness, although the validation data was not augmented in the same manner. On the held-out test set, balanced performance was achieved by the proposed approach, with about 0.93 accuracy and a macro-F1 score close to 0.94 being obtained, while established baselines such as EfficientNet, Vision Transformer, and attention-based CNN models were outperformed under identical evaluation settings. Controlled ablations were used to isolate the contribution of MSCA and augmentation under the same training configuration. These results indicate that lightweight multi-scale contextual attention is effective for crop and pest recognition under realistic field conditions, although some visually similar classes remained difficult.

## 0.2 Attention mechanisms for the fine-grained recognition 122 

Field imagery task did not only solve the assignment of a label as a class but 123 

localisation of discriminative evidence in the scene as well. The distractors often 124 

included soil texture, shadows and the vegetation at the back of the models leading to 125 

the drift of attention to irrelevant parts. As a control to this effect, attention 126 

mechanisms were added to re-weight features and focus on informative regions including 127 

lesion clusters, pest presence, and discoloration patterns. The formulations of residual 128 

attention were incorporated in CNN backbones, where the feature refinement did not 129 

destabilise the optimisation. As a result, the response to minor symptoms was more 130 

sensitive, but the rejection of irrelevant texture was not always similar in all cases. 131 

Multi-scale processing was considered critical for fine-grained recognition. Target 132 

size and visibility were varied. The same condition was observed to appear differently 133 

across growth stages. Pyramid-style designs with adaptive feature fusion were explored 134 

to capture small or crowded targets across scales [7]. Related fusion modules were 135 

proposed for dense prediction tasks. For example TBSFF-style mechanisms were 136 

designed to combine multi-scale context with fine detail to improve segmentation 137 

quality [8]. In recognition settings discriminative feature responses were emphasized by 138 

channel attention. Background noise was reduced and localization of symptom patterns 139 

was improved by spatial or coordinate attention. A SqueezeNext variant using 140 

multi-scale kernels with coordinate attention was reported to produce fewer false 141 

positives and sharper delineation of affected regions [9]. Collectively attention 142 

mechanisms were shown to improve feature focus and robustness to scale variation. 143 

However, the individual contribution of these components was often difficult to assess as 144 

the datasets splits and training protocols were varied across studies. 145

## 0.4 Summary of gaps and motivation 169 

As observed in literature, two gaps are repeatedly observed in practical field settings. 170 

First, controlled comparisons under identical data splits and training protocols are often 171 

not reported. Because of this, performance gains are sometimes attributed to 172 

architectural components even when the protocol was different. Second, 173 

deployment-oriented claims are frequently made without reporting model size or 174 

parameter count or inference latency. As a result, reproducibility is claimed while 175 

practical justification is not fully supported. 176 

A unified multi-class formulation is emphasized in the present work. A lightweight 177 

multi-scale contextual attention refinement is also emphasized. A controlled empirical 178 

evaluation is conducted to isolate the effects of attention design and data augmentation. 179 

However, these effects are isolated under a fixed and reproducible protocol, which may 180 

still vary across experiments. 181 

# Methodology 182 

This section describes the pipeline that was utilized for building and evaluating the 183 

proposed crop–condition recognition system. A curated dataset of 21,404 field-style 184 

images spanning 15 labels was assembled and was quality-checked during preprocessing. 185 

Reproducibility was treated as a first-class requirement while designing the workflow. 186 

For that reason, the dataset was split into training, validation, and a held-out test set 187 

before any augmentation, using a fixed random seed and stratified sampling [14]. At the 188 

outset, before any training was carried out, resizing and normalization of images were 189 

performed. Only on the training split was augmentation applied, with field variability 190 

being emulated, while evaluation performance was not being inflated. 191 

In the core design, a ResNet-50 feature extractor was being used, augmented with 192 

the Multi-Scale Contextual Attention (MSCA) module, and followed by a lightweight 193 

classification head, producing 15-way predictions. Based on validation performance, 194 

hyperparameters were selected. Once on the frozen test set, final performance was 195 

reported, using accuracy, macro-precision, macro-recall, macro-F1, and Matthews 196 

Correlation Coefficient (MCC) [15]. In Figure 1, the end-to-end workflow was 197 

summarized, while the overall processing stages were being illustrated. 198

## 0.7 Model architecture 281 

The proposed architecture contains three components. First is a ResNet-50 backbone. 282 

Second is the MSCA attention module. Third is a lightweight classification head that 283 

produces logits for 15 classes. The ordering is simple, although the attention block is 284 

placed inside the backbone feature stream. 285 

0.7.1 Backbone: ResNet-50 feature extractor 286 

ResNet-50 was adopted to extract discriminative features while keeping computational 287 

cost moderate [22]. ImageNet-pretrained weights were used for transferring generic 288 

visual structure, and the network was then fine-tuned for adapting to field conditions 289 

such as clutter illumination shifts, and partial occlusion [23]. Inputs were resized to 290 

256 × 256 and were normalized using ImageNet statistics [18, 19]. 291 

The canonical ResNet-50 topology was retained throughout the experiments. A 7 × 7 292 

convolution with stride 2, followed by a 3 × 3 max-pooling operation, was used for 293 

forming the stem [24]. Four residual stages were then used for producing progressively 294 

lower-resolution feature maps. Intermediate features at 16 × 16 resolution were 295 

forwarded to the MSCA module, which was considered effective for preserving spatial 296 

detail. This stage was selected as providing a practical balance between spatial 297 

resolution and semantic richness, whereas earlier layers were considered too low-level, 298 

and later layers were observed to lose fine details due to repeated downsampling. Final 299 

8 × 8 features were globally pooled for obtaining a 2048-dimensional representation [25]. 300 

In a few preliminary runs, features from earlier stages were considered, but were not 301 

retained due to unstable behavior being observed. 302 

Fine-tuning was performed in two stages. First, the backbone was frozen and only 303 

the head was trained. Second, upper stages were unfrozen gradually with a smaller 304 

learning rate than the head. Weight decay and early stopping were used. Checkpointing 305

0.7.2 Attention: Multi-Scale Contextual Attention (MSCA) 309 

MSCA is designed to improve symptom-focused learning under scale variation and 310 

clutter. Diagnostic cues are emphasized while stable optimization is preserved through 311 

residual fusion [28]. While the components are related to established multi-scale and 312 

attention mechanisms, the novelty here lies in their lightweight integration within a 313 

standard CNN and in the controlled evaluation that isolates their contribution under a 314 

fixed protocol. To further clarify the novelty of the proposed MSCA module, it is 315 

compared with attention mechanisms such as Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE), CBAM, and 316 

pyramid attention designs. 317 

The SE module focuses on channel recalibration. The spatial structure of the feature 318 

map is not explicitly considered in SE, and contextual aggregation is obtained only 319 

through global pooling. This improves channel selectivity but scale variation is not 320 

directly addressed in field imagery. 321 

The CBAM module extends SE by incorporating spatial attention after channel 322 

attention. However, the attention operations in CBAM are performed on features 323 

extracted at a single receptive-field scale. The contextual information was therefore 324 

being limited to the inherent scale of the backbone feature map. In complex agricultural 325 

scenes, where lesions and pests were appearing at different sizes and densities, such 326 

single-scale processing was often being insufficient, even when deeper backbones were 327 

being used. 328

The proposed MSCA module was differentiated through three aspects. First, explicit 334

different receptive-field sizes being obtained simultaneously and fused before attention 337 

refinement. Second, joint channel and spatial attention was being applied on the fused 338 

representation, rather than being applied sequentially. Third, refined features were being 339 

integrated with the original backbone features through residual fusion, with the original 340 

signal being preserved and stable optimization being supported during fine-tuning. 341 

The novelty of MSCA was therefore being defined by the efficient combination of 342 

parallel multi-scale context extraction, lightweight joint attention refinement, and 343 

residual integration within a standard ResNet backbone. The objective was not framed 344

## 0.10 Inference and deployment-oriented profiling 395 

At the inference time every input image was resized to a square size of 256 × 256, 396 

transformed into the RGB colour space, and normalised using the same statistical 397 

parameters used in the training. A ResNet-50 backbone, Multi-Scale Contextual 398 

Attention (MSCA) module, global average pooling layer and a classification head were 399 

then applied to the pre-processed tensor, producing a probability distribution over 15 400 

classes. In order to improve throughput in a real-world situation, a batch inference on a 401 

graphics card was conducted. Unless indicated in an ablation study, test-time 402 

augmentation was not used at all [32]. Uninterrupted preprocessing is required when 403 

deployed; inconsistencies in the required pipeline would modify the reported 404 

performance measures. 405 

To facilitate the arguments about the suitability of deployment, the computational 406 

characteristics were profiled with the hardware configuration in Table 4. The overall 407 

number of parameters was taken in the model summary, and latency was computed as 408 

the median execution time in several forward passes after a warm-up period without 409 

data-loading delays. Standard profiling tools estimated the floating-point operations 410 

(FLOPS) of variants of the model with a fixed input resolution of (256 × 256). To control 411 

comparability, measurements were made on the same software stack and preprocessing 412 

pipeline. The given accuracies were also provided with the latency and FLOP numbers, 413 

thus, correlating recognition performance with the deployability, but variability over run 414 

time and hardware environment was still and could not be controlled completely. 415

# Results and Discussions 434 

The proposed universal crop condition recognition system was tested on the real field 435 

images. A ResNet- 50 backbone was used with the MSCA module in the inference 436 

pipeline and it was trained on 21404 images of fifteen semantic categories. There was no 437 

control of visual conditions, cluttered backgrounds, partial occlusions and fluctuating 438 

illumination in most samples; a number of classes were harder to identify due to tiny or 439 

low-contrast manifestations of symptoms. 440 

A held-out test split was fixed and not observed during training and hyperparameter 441 

optimization and only the last checkpoint was chosen based on validation macro-F1. 442 

Despite the fact that more robust statistical approximations might have been achieved 443 

through cross-validation or repeated random splits, they were considered less 444 

representative of the deployment situations, in which a model is trained once and tested 445 

once using an unknown set. 446 

Robustness was tested by stable dynamics of learning, and controlled ablation studies 447 

which performed under the same split and random seed. The comparative framework 448 

therefore enabled analysis of model variants whilst maintaining a realistic deployment 449 

scenario. Among the reported results are learning dynamics, held-out performance, 450 

MSCA and augmentation ablations, class-wise behaviour on a balanced diagnostic 451 

subset, backbone-depth comparisons, deployment-oriented profiling and an indicative 452 

comparison to recent literature, but not all these aspects were given equal weighting. 453

## 0.16 Statistical significance analysis 531 

To determine whether the model behavior was consistent as opposed to stochastic 532

observed improvements were a reflection of the model behavior or not. The reason 534

## 0.22 Summary, limitations, and practical implications 663 

Overall, the main design choice is supported by the reported results. Under a controlled 664 

protocol, consistent improvements are yielded by MSCA over the non-attention baseline, 665 

while robust invariances under field variability are actively learned through 666 

augmentation. Where performance is weaker is clarified by the class-wise diagnostic 667 

analysis, with recall reductions being concentrated in cases involving small or 668 

low-contrast symptoms. In unconstrained field imagery, such failure modes are expected. 669 

Several limitations are also acknowledged. From a single curated source, all images were 670 

obtained, and cross-dataset or cross-region evaluation was not performed; therefore, 671 

performance under unseen geographic regions, imaging devices, or seasonal variation 672 

cannot be directly quantified. As image-level classification, the task is formulated, and 673 

pixel-level or region-level annotations are not available, which limits quantitative 674 

evaluation of localization accuracy and attention alignment. Across pest or condition 675 

categories, some labels were aggregated; granularity was reduced by this choice, but 676 

sufficient sample sizes and annotation reliability were preserved. The findings are not 677 

invalidated by these constraints, although directions for future work are clearly 678 

motivated. An important deployment concern remains generalization beyond the 679 

evaluated dataset. Substantial variation is exhibited by field imagery in lighting, 680 

background clutter, occlusion patterns, crop varieties, and acquisition devices. Although 681 

diverse field-style images are included, explicit external evaluation was not conducted. 682 

The reported results should therefore be interpreted as evidence of robustness within 683 

the current data domain rather than as a guarantee of cross-domain transfer, and future 684

# Conclusion 699 

An image-based system for unified crop and pest/condition recognition was evaluated 700 

under realistic field conditions, using a ResNet-50 backbone augmented with a 701 

lightweight Multi-Scale Contextual Attention (MSCA) module and trained on 21,404 702 

images across 15 classes with a fixed, leakage-aware protocol. During training, stable 703 

convergence was observed, and clarity was improved through more explicit description 704 

of the MSCA design, dataset preparation, and evaluation strategy. On the held-out test 705 

set, an accuracy of 0.93 and a macro-F1 of 0.94 were achieved, with precision and recall 706 

remaining closely aligned. Under identical settings, controlled ablations demonstrated 707 

consistent gains over the non-attention baseline, and from the backbone-depth 708 

comparison it was evident that ResNet-50 outperformed shallower variants. Class-wise 709 

behavior was largely even. Lower recall nevertheless appeared in a small number of 710 

labels involving tiny, faint, or occluded symptoms, where attention sometimes diffused 711 

toward veins or uniform texture regions rather than focusing sharply. Suitable for 712 

decision support in field scouting is the overall pipeline, which also compares favorably 713 

with laboratory-focused studies. Still, it remains limited by the absence of cross-dataset 714 

evaluation and explicit localization, motivating future work on targeted augmentation, 715 

higher-resolution inference, and broader regional validation. 716
