# PlanktonFlow : hands-on deep-learning classification of plankton images for biologists 

# Hugo Walter 1, Caroline Gorzerino 1, Marc Collinet 1,Béatrice Porcon 1, François Martignac 1, and Eric Edeline 1

# https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.09.19.677346 

# Abstract 

High throughput image-acquisition devices tremendously increase our capacity to ob-serve biodiversity. However, for many biologists, the high-performance deep learning models that are needed to make biological sense out of very-large image sets remain difficult to implement. To fill this gap in biologists’ toolkit, we developed PlanktonFlow, a Python pipeline that streamlines the automation of plankton-image taxonomic assignment. PlanktonFlow makes it easy for inexperienced users to run a whole sequence of (i) automated image pre-processing and augmentation of rare classes, (ii) training up to four different high-performance convolution neural networks (CNNs: ResNet, DenseNet, EfficientNet, and YOLO), (iii) computing model classification-performance metrics so as to choose the best-performing model, and (iv) running inference on novel image sets. PlanktonFlow further includes routines to easily fine tune model hyper-parameters and optimize model’s performances. Using a tutorial style, we demonstrate the usage of PlanktonFlow to analyse freshwater-plankton images produced with the FlowCAM, comparing the relative clas-sification performances of the four optimized CNN architectures. For a baseline com-parison with a reference tool used by plankton biologists, we further assessed the clas-sification performances of the EcoTaxa web-service when used without any eye valida-tion in a pure-prediction mode. In line with a previous study on a benchmark plankton dataset, we found that EfficientNet-B5 achieved the highest macro-averaged F1 Score, outperforming other CNN models, which all surpassed EcoTaxa. Hyper-parameter op-timization was key to improving model performances. To ease an appropriation and further developments by the community, PlanktonFlow is open source, comes with a detailed documentation, and has a modular structure. We foresee that future work could integrate new deep-learning architectures (e.g., vision transformers, semi-supervised learning), and test the pipeline on images produced by other devices or from other taxonomic groups. 

Keywords: Computer vision, Image classification, Python, Plankton Classification.

Introduction 2

High-throughput biodiversity monitoring, as enabled by modern automatic sensors, provides 3

us with an unprecedented capacity to track rich, multidimensional data streams and, ultimately, 4

to accurately understand and forecast ecological dynamics across broad spatial and temporal 5

scales (Besson et al., 2022, Weinstein, 2018). In aquatic systems, a diversity of imaging-in-flow 6

and camera systems now capture a wide spectrum of plankton sizes and taxonomic diversity. 7

Submersible, high-frequency instruments like the Imaging FlowCytobot (Olson and Sosik, 2007), 8

the Dual Scripps Plankton Camera (DSPC, Merz et al., 2021) or the Underwater Vision Profiler 9

(UVP, Picheral et al., 2010) operate autonomously in situ to capture continuous records of plank-10 

ton. In parallel, benchtop imaging flow cytometers for plankton, such as the FlowCAM ® (Sieracki 11 

et al., 1998) or the PlanktoScope (Pollina et al., 2022), are widely used to analyse water samples 12 

ex-situ in the laboratory or under shipboard settings. 13 

However, the volume and heterogeneity of images produced by these machines often ex-14 

ceed the routine-processing capacity of traditional analysis workflows, and thus create bottle-15 

necks that hinder scientific progress. Softwares shipped with commercial instruments (for ex-16 

ample, FlowCAM’s VisualSpreadsheet) typically require substantial manual curation and make 17 

it impossible to process large datasets (Owen et al., 2022). To scale up classification, the Lab-18 

oratoire d’Océanographie de Villefranche developed EcoTaxa, a web-based platform intended 19 

for computer-assisted image classification, which is now the standard tool used by biologists for 20 

plankton analysis (Irisson et al., 2022; Picheral et al., 2025). 21 

EcoTaxa provides a user-friendly collaborative graphical interface. Based on a validated dataset, 22 

EcoTaxa predicts an object class using random forests fed with multiple image features, includ-23 

ing convolution neural networks (CNNs)-extracted features (see Figure S1, Walter et al., 2025). 24 

Predicted classes are then intended to be eye-validated. However, when projects are so large 25 

that they surpass human-validation capacities, one may use EcoTaxa without any further eye 26 

validation in a "pure-prediction mode", which consists of either using directly the classification 27 

provided by random forests, or of retaining only images with the highest random-forest pre-28 

diction score (Faillettaz et al., 2016). Unfortunately however, EcoTaxa lacks prediction-quality 29 

metrics (accuracy, recall, confusion matrix...), making it difficult to assess its performances in a 30 

pure-prediction mode. 31 

At the same time, recent studies show on various benchmark plankton datasets that mod-32 

ern deep-learning approaches (CNNs, Vision Transformers) can achieve classification accuracies 33 

that potentially surpass those achieved by EcoTaxa (Kyathanahally et al., 2021, Kyathanahally 34 

et al., 2022, Eerola et al., 2024). However, several obstacles prevent the adoption of these novel 35 

methods by EcoTaxa users. The codes and data used to run novel methods are sometimes pub-36 

licly available, but these scripts may run proprietary software (Lumini and Nanni, 2019), and/or 37 

their documentation may be too limited for beginners to be able to adapt the scripts to their 38 

own needs (e.g., Kyathanahally et al., 2021, Kraft et al., 2022, Kyathanahally et al., 2022). In fact, 39 

the computational/engineering overhead required to train, deploy and maintain robust models 40 

in operational settings are often beyond-of-reach for non-specialists. Another hindrance to the 41 

reutilization of published scripts is their frequent lack of routines for image pre-treatment (e.g., 42 

augmentation of rare classes) or performance measurements (but see Maracani et al., 2023). Yet, 43 

performance comparison among architectures is crucial because variation among datasets often 44 

precludes a direct transfer of methods among instruments and environments, a problem known 45

To demonstrate the usage of PlanktonFlow, we here implement the whole pipeline on a 63 

dataset of labelled plankton images from our own freshwater mesocosm experiments. Images of 64 

ethanol-fixed samples were produced using the FlowCAM, and were manually-labelled using Ex-65 

oTaxa and a single eye validation. We deliberately chose not to work with a benchmark dataset, 66 

because benchmarks are less likely to represent the whole diversity of object classes encoun-67 

tered under operational conditions. Compared to benchmark datasets, our custom dataset was 68 

maybe more difficult to classifify, which would decrease the absolute performances of classifiers, 69 

but would not influence their relative performances. 70 

As a baseline for performance comparison, we use EcoTaxa in a pure-prediction mode, so as 71 

to test for an expected added value of modern classifiers compared to a standard classification 72

Material and methods 74 

Dataset 75 

Raw dataset. The plankton image dataset used in this study was acquired from a long-term meso-76 

cosm experiment on the effects of invasive crayfish on freshwater ecosystems (Dézerald et al., 77 

2023). 12 outdoor, fishless mesocosms were installed in 2020 at the PEARL, the INRAE experi-78 

mental platform for aquatic ecology and ecotoxicology (U3E) located in Rennes, France (Platform 79 

PEARL). The mesocosms were initially seeded with pond sediments, such that a rich vegetal and 80 

animal community rapidly developed. Organisms were also free to colonise mesocosms by aerial 81 

dispersal. In 2021, red swamp crayfish ( Procambarus clarkii ) were introduced in eight mesocosms 82 

and soon eradicated all the vegetation. Monthly water samples were taken from the 12 meso-83 

cosms in 2022 and 2023 (24 months). Hence, our image dataset contained diverse plankton taxa 84 

from both vegetated and non-vegetated natural freshwater ecosystems. 85 

Imaging was performed using the FlowCAM (Fluid Imaging Technologies Inc.), a high-throughput 86 

imaging cytometer designed for particle analysis in liquid media. In image-based studies, the tax-87 

onomic resolution attainable by either humans or deep-learning methods critically depends on 88

A critical preprocessing step in the pipeline involved detecting and removing scale bars that 126 

were embedded in some of the original images. Inconsistency in the presence of scale bars was 127 

due to varying settings in our use of the different versions of ZooProcess. To prevent data leakage 128 

due to the model learning scale bars rather than biological features, we implemented an auto-129 

mated pipeline to remove scale bars. Initial tests using traditional computer vision techniques 130 

(template matching) proved unreliable due to slight inter-image variability in the scale bar’s pixel 131 

rendering and contrast. We therefore fine-tuned a YOLOv8 object detection model for this task. 132 

On a held-out test set (N=160), the model achieved 100% precision and recall, with zero false 133 

positives or false negatives. Since scale bars in our images did not overlap with the plankton 134 

objects, this perfect detection rate ensured that all artifacts were completely removed without 135 

compromising biological data. 136 

Finally, the pre-processed image set was split into three disjoint subsets in a stratified manner 137 

across the 76 classes, ensuring that rare classes remained proportionally represented similarly 138 

across all subsets: 70% for training (90,723 images), 20% for validation (25,921 images), and 10% 139 

for testing (12,961 images). 140 

Data Augmentation. The performances of deep learning models often deteriorate when exposed 141 

to imbalanced training distributions, especially in multi-class settings with sparse and heteroge-142 

neous categories. In the context of our plankton classification task, pronounced class imbalance 143 

was observed across taxa, primarily due to natural ecological abundance gradients. To mitigate 144 

this issue and to enhance the model’s generalization capability, we implemented a class-aware 145 

data augmentation strategy aimed at selectively enriching under-represented categories in the 146 

training dataset. 147 

Specifically, we augmented data using the Albumentations library (Buslaev et al., 2018), 148 

where each transformation is applied independently with a given probability. The transforma-149 

tions included horizontal flipping (50%), vertical flipping (20%), 90-degree rotation (30%), bright-150 

ness and contrast adjustment (40%), and hue/saturation modulation (30%). Since these opera-151 

tions are sampled separately, multiple augmentations may be applied to the same image. 152 

To standardise the training distribution, we defined an augmentation threshold of 1,500 im-153 

ages per class. For any class below this threshold, we calculated the required number of synthetic 154

Model architectures 164 

The problem of dataset shifts makes it crucial to evaluate different deep-learning architec-165 

tures on the dataset at hand (Chen et al., 2025). To illustrate PlanktonFlow’s capabilities on this 166 

task, we compared the classification performances of four different CNN models, all of which are 167 

enclosed in PlanktonFlow: ResNet (He et al., 2016), DenseNet (Huang et al., 2018), EfficientNet 168 

(Tan and Le, 2020) and YOLOv11 (Khanam and Hussain, 2024). 169 

We chose ResNet in our candidate-model set for its use of residual connections, which fa-170 

cilitates gradient flow and enables the training of deep networks without degradation. Variants 171 

like ResNet-50 offer a solid balance between accuracy and resource use, making them a com-172 

mon baseline for transfer learning. DenseNet connects each layer to every other layer in a feed-173 

forward manner, improving gradient propagation and promoting feature reuse. This architec-174 

ture achieves strong generalization with fewer parameters, making it well-suited for complex 175 

but data-limited tasks. EfficientNet applies compound scaling to jointly optimize network depth, 176 

width, and input resolution, achieving high accuracy with fewer parameters and FLOPs (Float-177 

ing Point Operations, measure computational complexity). Its scalable variants (B0–B7) offer 178 

flexibility between speed and performance, with smaller models enabling fast prototyping. Fi-179 

nally, YOLO is a multi-task CNN that is advertised by its deveopers to excel at object detection, 180 

tracking, instance segmentation, image classification, and pose estimation tasks 1. We included 181 

YOLOV11 in our panel of CNNs because it is arguably the most user-friendly deep learning 182 

workflow, and as such it is easily accessible to beginners. 183 

For a baseline comparison with the standard tool used by many plankton biologists, we fur-184 

ther measured the classification performances provided by EcoTaxa in a pure-prediction mode 185 

withtout any further human validation. All four deep-learning architectures were subject to the 186 

exact same training, validation and testing procedures with Dtrain , Dval and Dtest , respectively 187 

(see Supplementary Information, Walter et al., 2025). In contast, because the platform makes it 188 

impossible to have a validation step, EcoTaxa was trained on Dtrain with deep features extracted 189 

using the zoocam_2022_04_06 model, and tested on Dval and Dtest grouped together. 190

Model optimization strategies 235 

All four CNN models were initialized by their developers with weights pre-trained on Ima-236 

geNet, a large and generic dataset of non-planktonic images. Classically, initialization precedes 237 

a second step of model fine-tuning on a specialized dataset, an appraoch known as transfer 238 

learning from an out-of-domain source. Transfer learning has been demonstrated to be highly 239 

effective for plankton classification (Maracani et al., 2023). However, classic fine-tuning based 240 

on standard loss functions tends to favour majority classes and may fail to generalize to rare 241 

or difficult classes, which is a problem for plankton that includes many rare classes as well as 242 

classes that may overlap greatly in morphology. 243 

To address this problem, we augmented classic fine tuning with extra optimization steps, 244 

including specialized loss functions to handle class imbalance and challenging samples, as well 245 

as hyperparameter tuning to identify optimal learning configurations (see below). Specifically, 246 

we optimized ResNet, DenseNet and EfficientNet, but we did not optimize YOLO as we chose 247 

this architecture to demonstrate the performances of an out-of-the-box deep-learning solution. 248 

EcoTaxa does not allow any model optimization by the user. 249

Hyperparameters: the knobs that control learning. Hyperparameters are parameters that define 282 

the configuration of the learning process but are not directly learned from the data. They play a 283 

critical role in determining how effectively a model can be trained and how well it generalizes to 284 

unseen data. Conceptually, hyperparameters can be thought of as ‘knobs’ that adjust how the 285 

model learns, analogous to changing the focus when examining a specimen under a microscope. 286 

Key hyperparameters include : 287 

• Learning rate: the step size used to update the model’s parameters at each iteration, 288 

affecting convergence speed and stability. 289 

• Batch size: the number of images processed before updating the model parameters, which 290 

influences the variance of gradient estimates and memory usage. 291

Per-class analysis 349 

While overall performance metrics provide a useful high-level summary, they often obscure 350 

important class-level behaviors—particularly in imbalanced datasets like ours. This section inves-351

the evaluated models. 353 

Class Support vs. F1 Score. To investigate whether class frequency impacted model performance, 354 

we used linear regressions of per-class F1 Scores against their corresponding support. As shown 355 

in Figure 2, regression lines were nearly flat for all models, and adjusted R2 were low, indicating 356 

almost no explanatory power. This shows that class frequency alone was not a strong predictor 357 

of classification quality, and suggests that the PlanktonFlow procedure was efficient at alleviating 358 

the classification problems that arise from class imbalance (a formal test of this hypothesis would 359 

require to run the analysis without the pre-processing procedure). 360

Discussion 386 

Our results demonstrated the capabilities of PlanktonFlow for the implementation of a com-387 

plete deep-learning pipeline, including a performance comparison among multiple models (for a 388 

hands-on tutorial on how to reproduce the results, see the next section). On our custom dataset, 389 

the best overall performance was achieved by EfficientNet-B5, while both ResNet and DenseNet 390 

achieved comparable results within a 1-2% margin. YOLO was lagging behind, illustrating the lim-391 

its of out-of-the-box solutions when they are not optimized on the dataset at hand. Finally, our 392

Typical workflow with the pipeline. PlanktonFlow is entirely configuration-driven using dedicated 417 

YAML configuration files, in which detailed explanations are provided in the form of comments. 418 

Using these comments, users are expected to adapt these files to their own dataset and exper-419 

imental design. This ensures full reproducibility while keeping commands themselves simple. A 420 

typical workflow involves a sequence of preprocessing, training, and inference. Each of these 421 

three operations has its own YAML configuration file and may be performed independently. 422 

Data preprocessing. PlanktonFlow accommodates three common data formats: 423 

(1) Hierarchical dataset : classical folder-based structure with one folder per image class. 424 

(2) Simple CSV/TSV mapping : one single folder containing all images mixed together, accom-425 

panied by a basic, user-generated two-column CSV/TSV file simply mapping each image 426 

filename to its class label. 427 

(3) EcoTaxa export : a specialized parser designed to directly ingest the complex, multi-column 428 

TSV archives exported by the EcoTaxa web platform. It automatically navigates the EcoTaxa-429 

specific metadata structure (e.g., extracting labels from the object_annota 430 

tion_category column) to associate classes with images, eliminating the need for man-431 

ual file reformatting by the user. 432 

Users can customize preprocessing through configuration files to: 433 

• Prune classes based on a minimum and maximum number of images. 434 

• Perform data augmentation to balance the dataset. 435 

• Optionally remove scale bars using a pre-trained YOLOv8 model. 436 

• Automatically split data into training, validation, and test sets. 437 

At the end of this step, the dataset should be ready to train a model with. 438 

Training. Training is fully configurable via YAML files. Users can select a model architecture, 439 

model variants, loss functions (e.g., focal loss, label smoothing, weighted cross-entropy), opti-440 

mizers etc. Metrics are tracked in real-time, either locally or online through Weights & Biases 441 

(W&B). Pre-configured hyperparameters have been validated on our plankton dataset, allow-442 

ing FlowCAM users to achieve good performance out-of-the-box. However, PlanktonFlow also 443 

makes it possible to optimize hyperparameters on your own dataset. 444 

Hyperparameter optimization. Hyperparameter optimization in PlanktonFlow operates W&B, which 445 

provides an efficient and reproducible framework for exploring large hyperparameter spaces. In-446 

stead of requiring the user to manually test parameters such as learning rate, weight decay, batch 447 

size, or optimizer type, the pipeline automatically launches a series of training runs with different 448 

configurations sampled from a predefined search space (specified in the YAML sweep configu-449 

ration file). The underlying algorithm in W&B prioritizes promising regions of the search space, 450 

thereby reducing the number of runs needed compared to a naive grid search. 451 

From the PlanktonFlow user’s perspective, initiating a sweep requires only pointing to the 452 

provided sweep configuration file. The sweep will continue to launch successive training jobs 453 

until it is manually stopped. Typically, users are advised to monitor validation metrics in the 454 

W&B dashboard and stop the sweep once these metrics show signs of stable convergence across 455 

runs. At this point, the best-performing hyperparameter set can be automatically exported into 456 

a dedicated YAML configuration file for further training or inference. PlanktonFlow also saves 457

Conclusion 495 

A major strength of PlanktonFlow is its high degree of automation and scalability: all core 496 

stages, from data preprocessing to training and inference, are fully automated, minimizing the 497 

need for human intervention and making the approach highly suitable for large-scale or continu-498 

ous monitoring workflows. Also, although the models were trained on a specific plankton dataset, 499 

the underlying architecture and modular structure of the pipeline promote generalizability. With 500 

only minimal changes to the input format or preprocessing configuration, PlanktonFlow could 501 

be adapted to other image-acquisition devices or taxonomic groups. Our hope is that the com-502
