# Abstract 

Accurate quantification of leaf lesion severity is essential for plant disease research and phenotyping but is often limited by subjective visual scoring and time-intensive manual image analysis. We present LIME, a fully automated, open-source image analysis pipeline for high-throughput quantification of leaf lesions from disease assay images. LIME integrates zero-shot leaf segmentation using the Segment Anything Model with a convolutional neural network for lesion area estimation. Applied to Arabidopsis thaliana leaves infected with Sclerotinia sclerotiorum , the proposed approach achieved a mean absolute percentage error of 12.9%, comparable to observed intrarater variability in manual scoring. Stratified evaluation across lesion-size groups demonstrated consistent prediction accuracy for small, intermediate, and large lesions, and comparative analysis showed that the deep learning–based model substantially outperformed color-based baseline methods. Under GPU-accelerated execution, LIME processed complete assays containing approximately 200 leaves in 15 minutes, representing an approximate 13-fold reduction in processing time relative to manual annotation. April 29, 2026 1/26 Together, these results indicate that LIME enables objective, reproducible, and scalable quantification of leaf lesion severity in standardized plant pathology assays. The pipeline is released as an open-source tool to support quantitative phenotyping studies. April 29, 2026 2/26 Introduction 1

Plants are frequently attacked by pathogenic microorganisms in both natural and agricultural systems, 2

leading to observable disease symptoms such as chlorotic or necrotic leaf lesions. Accurate quantification of 3

disease severity is essential for several reasons: i) it enables the linkage between disease progression and yield 4

loss; ii) it supports the evaluation of germplasm and cultivars in plant breeding programs; iii) it informs 5

disease management strategies such as targeted pesticide application during epidemics [1]. In addition, 6

quantitative disease assessment contributes to a deeper understanding of fundamental biological processes, 7

including host–pathogen coevolution and plant disease epidemiology [1]. 8

Disease severity and host resistance can be assessed using a variety of approaches, including 9

measurements of bacterial load [2] and fungal load [3], detection of reactive oxygen species (ROS) [4], 10 

quantification of marker gene expression [5], and visual assessment of leaf symptoms [6]. Among these 11 

methods, visual assessment of leaf symptoms remains particularly attractive due to its conceptual simplicity, 12 

minimal training requirements, and relatively high throughput [6]. Depending on the pathogen and host, 13 

symptoms may manifest as chlorosis, as observed in Arabidopsis thaliana infected with Pseudomonas 14 

syringae pv. tomato DC3000, or as necrotic lesions, such as those caused by necrotrophic fungi including 15 

Sclerotinia sclerotiorum and Botrytis cinerea . 16 

Historically, macroscopic disease symptoms have often been evaluated using ordinal rating scales, or 17 

disease indices, which categorize symptom severity from minimal to severe [1]. While widely adopted, these 18 

assessments rely heavily on human judgment and are therefore subject to observer bias, interrater and 19 

intrarater variability, and limited scalability. As a result, there is growing demand for objective, quantitative, 20 

and reproducible methods for assessing disease symptoms at scale [7]. 21 

On the other hand, once individual leaves have been identified and isolated from assay images, a 22 

subsequent task is to quantify disease severity. In plant pathology, severity is most commonly assessed by 23 

measuring the proportion of leaf area exhibiting visible disease symptoms [8]. Even when performed by 24 

experienced specialists, this process is challenging, as evaluators must visually interpret lesion boundaries 25 

and align observations with diagnostic guidelines. As a result, manual assessments inherently involve 26 

subjectivity, and the resulting annotations cannot be regarded as absolute ground truth [8]. This subjectivity 27 

gives rise to both intrarater variability, reflecting differences when the same annotator repeats measurements, 28 

and interrater variability, reflecting disagreement across annotators. 29 

To meet this demand, image-based approaches have become increasingly common for quantifying fungal 30 

lesions, particularly those involving necrotic tissue. Manual or semi-automated workflows based on tools such 31 

3/26 as ImageJ remain widely used [9, 10]. Although these tools enable pixel-based lesion area estimation and 32 

diameter measurements, they are time-intensive and often struggle to robustly quantify irregular lesion 33 

morphologies [6]. Alternative solutions, including dedicated hardware such as the YMJ-C smart leaf area 34 

meter [11] and commercial software such as Adobe Photoshop [12], can partially alleviate these challenges 35 

but frequently incur higher costs or require substantial manual intervention [6]. 36 

More recently, several image analysis tools have been developed to streamline disease quantification. 37 

Examples include PIDIQ [13], which focuses on pathogen-induced chlorosis, Colour-analyzer [6], a web-based 38 

platform for fungal lesion quantification, and Sc-Analyzer [7], which employs a flatbed scanner and 39 

grid-based layout to estimate pathogen spread. However, many existing tools remain semi-automated, 40 

require specialized imaging equipment, or depend on manually tuned color thresholds, limiting their 41 

robustness and reproducibility across experimental conditions [6]. 42 

In this context, there is a clear need for fully automated, reproducible pipelines that can robustly quantify 43 

leaf lesions across large-scale assays while minimizing human intervention. In this study, we focus on lesions 44 

caused by the necrotrophic fungus Sclerotinia sclerotiorum in Arabidopsis thaliana , a pathosystem commonly 45 

used to study host resistance mechanisms. This work supports broader efforts to develop transgenic plants 46 

with enhanced resistance to S. sclerotiorum , including strategies based on RNA interference targeting 47 

essential fungal genes or virulence factors [14]. 48 

Here, we introduce the Lesion Inference & Measurement Engine (LIME), an open-source, fully automated 49 

command-line tool for high-throughput quantification of lesion formation on plant leaves. LIME integrates a 50 

foundation-model-based segmentation stage with a deep learning regression model to estimate lesion area 51 

and total leaf area, enabling precise calculation of infected tissue proportions across hundreds of samples per 52 

assay. By eliminating manual thresholding and subjective scoring, LIME substantially reduces 53 

observer-dependent variability while providing throughput suitable for large-scale phenotyping studies. 54 

The main contributions of this work are as follows: i) We present a fully automated, end-to-end pipeline 55 

for quantitative leaf lesion measurement that requires no manual annotation or threshold tuning during 56 

inference. ii) We demonstrate the use of a zero-shot segmentation foundation model to robustly isolate 57 

individual leaves from high-density assay images under variable lighting conditions. iii) We introduce a deep 58 

learning–based lesion area estimation model that achieves accuracy comparable to human intrarater 59 

variability while providing substantial gains in throughput. iv) We release LIME as an open-source, 60 

reproducible tool designed for integration into standardized plant pathology workflows. 61 

4/26 Related work 62 

## Traditional lesion quantification methods 63 

Traditional approaches for assessing foliar disease severity, including visual rating scales and manual or 64 

semi-automated image analysis tools, are widely used in plant pathology and phenotyping. These methods, 65 

along with their limitations in terms of subjectivity, scalability, and dependence on manual parameter tuning, 66 

are discussed in detail in the Introduction. While these approaches reduce reliance on purely visual scoring, 67 

they typically require manual interaction, fixed thresholds, or specialized acquisition setups, which can limit 68 

throughput and reproducibility. 69 

## Segmentation methods in plant phenotyping 70 

Traditional segmentation approaches in vision-based plant phenotyping have largely relied on classical image 71 

processing techniques. ApLeaf [15], for example, employs grayscale thresholding to group pixels by intensity 72 

and separate foliage from background. Otsu’s method [16] automatically determines an optimal threshold by 73 

minimizing within-class variance and has been widely adopted for background subtraction in automated 74 

plant recognition systems [17, 18]. Another commonly used technique is the watershed transformation, which 75 

interprets an image as a topographic surface and segments regions by flooding from predefined seed points 76 

until strong gradient boundaries are encountered [19]. 77 

Despite their simplicity and efficiency, these methods are highly sensitive to imaging conditions. 78 

Variations in illumination intensity, angle, or spectral composition can substantially alter leaf 79 

appearance [20, 21], leading to inconsistent contrast, poorly defined boundaries, and reduced segmentation 80 

accuracy. Shadows, glare, and uneven lighting further complicate reproducible leaf delineation, limiting the 81 

robustness of both automated and semi-manual segmentation pipelines [20, 21]. 82 

Deep learning–based segmentation methods have demonstrated improved robustness under such 83 

variability. Convolutional neural network architectures such as U-Net [22] and DeepLab [23] learn 84 

hierarchical feature representations from annotated data and can reliably separate plant structures from 85 

background across a wide range of imaging conditions. However, the effectiveness of supervised segmentation 86 

models depends heavily on the availability of large, pixel-level annotated datasets, which are costly to 87 

produce and often unavailable for specialized plant pathology assays [24]. 88 

To mitigate this limitation, recent work has explored the use of large foundation models for segmentation. 89 

In this study, we leverage the Segment Anything Model (SAM) [25], a promptable segmentation model 90 

pretrained on the SA-1B dataset comprising over 11 million images and one billion masks. SAM provides 91 

5/26 strong zero-shot segmentation performance across diverse visual domains, making it well suited for scenarios 92 

where domain-specific annotated training data are limited. Rather than relying on fully supervised 93 

retraining, SAM is used here to generate candidate leaf masks that are subsequently refined using 94 

domain-specific constraints. 95 

## Computational approaches for leaf lesion assessment 96 

A variety of computational approaches have been proposed for automated disease spot detection and lesion 97 

quantification. Early image-processing methods often relied on color space transformations and handcrafted 98 

rules. Patil and Bodhe convert RGB images to the HSI color space and apply triangle-based thresholding to 99 

isolate diseased regions [26]. Pagola et al. [27] quantify nitrogen deficiency in barley leaves by manipulating 100 

RGB channels and applying principal component analysis (PCA) to derive pixel-wise greenness indices. 101 

Other approaches include fuzzy logic–based systems [28] and rule-based, knowledge-driven frameworks [29]. 102 

More recently, machine learning and deep learning methods have become increasingly prevalent for plant 103 

disease detection and quantification [24]. These approaches offer improved flexibility and robustness relative 104 

to handcrafted pipelines, particularly in the presence of variable lesion morphology, illumination conditions, 105 

and background complexity. Consequently, LIME adopts a deep learning–based framework for lesion severity 106 

estimation, enabling direct prediction of lesion area from image data while reducing reliance on manually 107 

defined thresholds or heuristics. 108

## LIME pipeline overview 110 

LIME is an automated image analysis pipeline for quantifying leaf lesions from high-resolution assay images. 111 

Given a single image containing multiple leaves arranged on a uniform background, the pipeline produces 112 

per-leaf measurements of total leaf area and lesion area. An overview of the workflow is shown in Fig. 1. 113 

The pipeline consists of four sequential stages. First, detached leaves exhibiting disease symptoms are 114 

collected and arranged on a uniform background to form a standardized assay image (Fig. 1A). Second, 115 

candidate segmentation masks are generated directly from the raw assay image using a pretrained, zero-shot 116 

segmentation model to identify potential leaf regions without manual prompts or annotations. These 117 

candidate masks are refined through an automated filtering process to remove background elements and 118 

produce a set of non-overlapping masks corresponding to individual leaves (Fig. 1B). Third, each segmented 119 

leaf is analyzed independently by a convolutional neural network to estimate lesion area, yielding per-leaf 120

## Assay setup and image acquisition 124 

Leaf lesion assays involved detached Sclerotinia-infected Arabidopsis thaliana leaves arranged on a uniform 125 

white background. The leaf infection experiment follows the methods of [3]. Each assay consisted of 126 

approximately 200 individual leaves placed in rows with minimal overlap between neighboring samples. 127 

Leaves were positioned to ensure clear visual separation and consistent orientation across the assay image; 128 

the color palette is a predefined set of RGB colors used to assign each mask group a distinct, consistent 129 

overlay color on the output image so humans can easily tell groups apart; the ruler is used for physical 130 

scaling when calculating the sizes of the lesion area. (Fig. 2A). 131 

Images were acquired using a digital camera mounted at a fixed height above the assay surface to 132 

maintain consistent imaging geometry across experiments. Camera position, focal length, and lighting 133 

7/26 conditions were kept constant within and across assays. A physical scale (ruler) was included within the field 134 

of view of each image to enable conversion from pixel-based measurements to physical units (Fig. 2 A). 135 

For experiments involving multiple genotypes or experimental conditions, leaves corresponding to each 136 

group were arranged in separate horizontal rows. A QR code encoding the sample identifier was placed at 137 

the beginning of each row. These QR codes were later used during post-processing to associate segmented 138 

leaves with their corresponding experimental groups. 139 

All images were captured at high resolution and stored in a lossless format prior to analysis. No manual 140 

cropping, thresholding, or image enhancement was performed before processing with LIME. The raw assay 141 

images served as the sole input to the pipeline. 142 

## Leaf segmentation 143 

SAM-based mask generation 144 

The first step of leaf segmentation in LIME is the generation of candidate object masks from the raw assay 145 

image. For this purpose, we employ the Segment Anything Model (SAM) [25], a promptable segmentation 146 

model pretrained on a large-scale, diverse image corpus. 147 

Given an input assay image, SAM is applied in a fully automatic manner to produce a set of candidate 148 

segmentation masks (Fig. 2 B). Each mask corresponds to a contiguous region of pixels that SAM identifies 149 

as a potential object. No user-defined prompts, bounding boxes, or point annotations are provided during 150 

inference. As a result, the output of this stage consists of a collection of overlapping and non-overlapping 151 

masks representing both leaf and non-leaf regions within the image. 152 

The generated masks are retained in their original resolution and treated as candidate regions for 153 

subsequent processing. At this stage, no assumptions are made regarding object class, and no filtering or 154 

thresholding is applied. The purpose of this stage is solely to enumerate potential leaf regions with high 155 

recall, deferring all selection and refinement decisions to the subsequent mask refinement step. 156 

Iterative mask refinement 157 

The candidate masks produced by SAM include both leaf and non-leaf regions and therefore require further 158 

refinement to isolate individual leaves. LIME performs this refinement through an iterative, color-based 159 

filtering procedure that progressively identifies masks corresponding to leaf tissue while rejecting background 160 

elements and spurious regions (Alg. 1). 161

## Lesion area estimation 180 

Following leaf segmentation, LIME estimates lesion area for each individual leaf using a convolutional neural 181 

network. This stage operates on cropped leaf images derived from the segmentation masks and produces a 182 

single scalar value corresponding to the predicted lesion area. 183

CNN architecture 184 

To estimate lesion size on each leaf, we adopt a modified NASNet A backbone. NASNet A—introduced by 185 

Zoph et al. [30] via Neural Architecture Search (NAS)—uses reinforcement learning to assemble two types of 186 

modular “cells”: normal cells, which preserve spatial dimensions for feature extraction, and reduction cells, 187 

which downsample feature maps while increasing their depth. Each cell is defined as a directed acyclic graph 188 

combining operations like depthwise separable convolutions, pooling layers, and skip connections. Thanks to 189 

its automated search process, NASNet A scales gracefully: cell structures optimized on small datasets (e.g., 190 

CIFAR 10) transfer effectively to large benchmarks such as ImageNet. In our pipeline, we load the ImageNet 191 

pretrained NASNet A, replace its classification head with a regression head, and train end-to-end using a 192 

mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) loss to predict per leaf lesion area. 193

## Post-processing and data export 210 

Following lesion area estimation, LIME performs a series of post-processing steps to convert pixel-based 211 

measurements into physical units, associate individual leaves with experimental metadata, and export results 212 

in structured formats. 213 

Total leaf area is computed for each segmented leaf by summing the pixels within its binary segmentation 214 

mask. Lesion area predictions produced by the regression model are also expressed in pixel units. To convert 215 

both measurements to physical units (e.g., mm 2 or cm 2), a calibration factor is computed from the in-frame 216 

physical scale included in each assay image. This factor represents the physical area per pixel and is applied 217 

uniformly across all measurements within the image. 218 

Our assays contain multiple experimental groups, and leaves are arranged in horizontal rows, each labeled 219 

with a QR code encoding the corresponding sample identifier. QR codes are detected by LIME and decoded 220 

from the assay image and used to identify the row associated with each experimental condition. Individual 221

with the number of clusters determined by the number of detected QR codes. This procedure enables 223 

automatic association of each leaf with its corresponding experimental group. 224 

Quantitative results are exported as comma-separated value (CSV) files, containing per-leaf 225

These outputs are the final result of the pipeline and are used for downstream statistical analysis and 229 

visualization. 230 

## Color-based baseline methods 231 

To provide interpretable and lightweight baselines for lesion quantification, we implemented two color-based 232 

lesion detection methods operating in the HSV color space. Both baselines estimate lesion area by identifying 233 

healthy leaf tissue and treating remaining leaf pixels as lesions. These approaches do not require learning 234 

during inference and rely only on color statistics derived from manually sampled data. 235 

HSV threshold-based lesion detection 236 

The first baseline uses fixed HSV thresholds to separate healthy leaf tissue from lesion regions. Input leaf 237 

images are first converted from RGB to HSV color space. Pixels corresponding to the white background are 238

Healthy and lesion regions exhibit distinct distributions in hue (A) and saturation (B). removed using a high-value, low-saturation threshold. All remaining pixels are treated as leaf tissue. 239 

Healthy leaf pixels are identified using empirically derived HSV thresholds obtained from manual color 240 

sampling of lesion and healthy regions. The samples are from 30 random leaf images from the training 241 

dataset. Fig. 5 shows the distribution of the hue, saturation and value levels of the sampled pixels. Based on 242 

these samples, healthy tissue is characterized by a narrow hue range corresponding to green leaf color, high 243 

saturation values, and moderate value intensity. Pixels falling within this predefined healthy range are 244 

classified as healthy leaf tissue. All other non-background pixels are classified as lesion candidates. 245 

To reduce noise and spurious detections, morphological opening and closing operations are applied to the 246 

lesion mask using a small structuring element. Connected components smaller than a minimum area 247 

threshold are subsequently removed. The final lesion area is computed as the number of pixels classified as 248 

lesion. 249 

K-means clustering-based lesion detection 250 

The second baseline employs unsupervised clustering to partition leaf pixels into color-consistent groups. 251 

After conversion to HSV color space, background pixels are excluded using the same white-background 252 

criterion as in the threshold-based method. The remaining pixels are clustered in HSV space using K-means 253 

clustering with a fixed number of clusters. 254 

Cluster centers are analyzed to identify the cluster corresponding to healthy leaf tissue. This 255 

identification is based on learned color characteristics of healthy leaves, namely high saturation and hue 256 

values within the expected green range. A composite score combining saturation magnitude and proximity of 257 

hue to the healthy-leaf mean is used to select the healthy cluster. 258 

All pixels not assigned to the healthy cluster are classified as lesion pixels. Lesion area is computed as the 259 

total number of pixels assigned to non-healthy clusters. No spatial smoothing or post-clustering 260 

morphological filtering is applied in this baseline. 261 

14/26 Area conversion and batch inference 262 

For both color-based baselines, lesion area is initially computed in pixel units for each individual leaf image. 263 

Pixel-based lesion areas are converted to physical units using the same image calibration factor applied in the 264 

main LIME pipeline. Baseline inference is performed independently for each leaf image, enabling direct 265 

comparison with the NASNet–based lesion estimation results. These baselines were evaluated using the same 266 

dataset, metrics, and calibration procedure as the LIME pipeline. 267 

## Evaluation metrics 268 

Model performance for lesion area estimation was evaluated using the MAPE, defined in Equation 1. MAPE 269 

was selected to provide a scale-normalized measure of prediction error across leaves exhibiting a wide range 270 

of lesion sizes. Reported performance values correspond to evaluation on the held-out test set. In addition, 271 

we also reported mean absolute error (MAE) for the model’s performance. 272 

## Hardware and runtime configuration 273 

All experiments were conducted using a workstation equipped with an NVIDIA RTX 3060 Ti GPU. GPU 274 

acceleration was used for both segmentation and lesion area estimation stages. While LIME can be executed 275 

on CPU-only systems, GPU acceleration is recommended due to the computational cost of segmentation 276 

models. 277 

Under this configuration, LIME processed a complete assay image containing approximately 200 leaves in 278 

approximately 15 minutes, including segmentation, lesion estimation, and post-processing steps. For 279 

comparison, manual lesion scoring required approximately 60 seconds per leaf, resulting in a total processing 280 

time of roughly 3 hours for an equivalent assay. 281

size, reflecting the larger absolute lesion areas in the intermediate and large groups. In contrast, MAPE 331 

decreased slightly as lesion size increased, indicating that relative prediction error was more pronounced for 332 

small lesions than for larger ones. 333 

The color-based baselines exhibited markedly different trends. Both HSV thresholding and K-means 334 

clustering produced very large MAE values across all lesion-size groups, indicating substantial absolute 335 

overestimation of lesion area. These errors were especially severe for small lesions, where MAPE exceeded 336 

100%, reflecting the sensitivity of relative error metrics to small denominators. While MAPE values for the 337 

baselines decreased for larger lesions, absolute errors remained high, suggesting that these methods 338 

consistently misclassified healthy tissue as lesion regardless of disease severity. 339 

Overall performance on the full test set followed similar patterns. The NASNet A-based model 340 

maintained stable relative accuracy across lesion sizes, achieving an overall MAPE of 12.9%, whereas both 341 

color-based baselines exhibited high absolute and relative errors and strong dependence on lesion size. 342

## Consistency and throughput comparison 346 

To assess the consistency of manual lesion scoring, a subset of 12 leaf images was randomly selected from the 347 

dataset and independently rescored by the same biologist. Fig. 8 demonstrates those rescored leaves and the 348 

evaluated lesion areas in pixels. Agreement between the original scores and the rescored values was 349 

quantified using MAPE. 350 

Across the 12 samples, the rescoring exhibited a mean absolute error of 83.3 pixels and a mean absolute 351 

percentage error of 10%. This intrarater error is close in magnitude to the overall LIME test-set MAPE of 352 

12.9% reported in Table 2, which helps contextualize model performance relative to human repeatability. 353 

These results indicate non-negligible intrarater variability in manual lesion quantification, particularly for 354 

leaves with smaller lesion areas. 355 

Runtime performance was assessed by measuring the total time required to process a complete assay 356 

image containing approximately 200 leaves. Under GPU-accelerated execution, the full LIME 357 

pipeline—including segmentation, lesion area estimation, and post-processing—required approximately 15 358 

minutes per assay. In contrast, manual lesion scoring required approximately 60 seconds per leaf, 359 

corresponding to a total processing time of roughly 3 hours for an equivalent assay. Based on these 360 

measurements, LIME provides an approximate 13-fold reduction in processing time compared to manual 361 

scoring for large-scale assays. Importantly, while the intrarater comparison was based on 12 leaves and does 362 

not directly quantify long-session fatigue effects, automated inference avoids fatigue- and attention-related 363 

drift that can affect repetitive manual visual scoring tasks as workload increases. 364

# Discussion 365 

## Interpretation of accuracy, lesion-size effects, and human variability 366 

Quantitative evaluation of lesion area estimation demonstrated that the NASNet A-based model achieved a 367 

MAPE of approximately 12.9% on the held-out test set. Stratified analysis across lesion-size groups further 368 

showed that prediction accuracy remained relatively stable for small, intermediate, and large lesions, with 369 

MAPE values ranging from 11.6% to 14.9%. In contrast, both color-based baselines exhibited substantially 370 

higher error and pronounced sensitivity to lesion size, particularly for small and intermediate lesions. 371 

These results indicate that the deep learning–based model generalizes more consistently across disease 372 

severity levels than threshold- or clustering-based approaches. The group-wise comparison highlights that 373 

color-based methods tend to overestimate lesion area when lesion appearance overlaps with healthy tissue 374 

coloration, an effect that becomes more pronounced for smaller lesions where absolute area differences 375 

21/26 translate into large relative errors. 376 

The intrarater rescoring indicates non-negligible variability even when annotations are performed by the 377 

same expert. This variability underscores the inherent subjectivity of manual lesion quantification and 378 

supports interpreting model error as agreement with human reference measurements rather than deviation 379 

from an objective ground truth. Within this context, the accuracy achieved by the NASNet A-based model 380 

falls within the range of variability observed in human scoring. 381 

## Practical implications for phenotyping pipelines 382 

Beyond predictive accuracy, the results demonstrate the practical advantages of a fully automated lesion 383 

quantification pipeline for large-scale leaf phenotyping studies. By eliminating manual threshold selection 384 

and subjective visual scoring, LIME reduces operator-dependent variability and ensures consistent 385 

measurement across experiments. This is particularly important in comparative phenotyping settings, where 386 

subtle differences in disease severity must be evaluated across large numbers of samples. 387 

The manual intrarater analysis (10% MAPE on 12 leaves) and the LIME test-set error (12.9% MAPE) 388 

indicate comparable accuracy in a small-scale repeated-scoring setting. However, the practical advantage of 389 

machine learning becomes stronger with larger cohorts: model performance is stable across throughput, 390 

whereas manual scoring quality can degrade during prolonged repetitive annotation due to human workload 391 

and attentional effects. In this sense, automation provides not only speed, but also consistency preservation 392 

as sample counts increase. 393 

Throughput analysis further highlights the scalability of the approach. Processing a complete assay image 394 

containing approximately 200 leaves required approximately 15 minutes under GPU-accelerated execution, 395 

compared to roughly 3 hours for manual scoring. This reduction in analysis time enables rapid iteration over 396 

large experimental cohorts and makes quantitative lesion analysis feasible in high-throughput experimental 397 

designs, such as genetic screens or multi-condition studies. 398 

The deterministic nature of the pipeline also ensures reproducibility and consistency compared with 399 

manual scoring: repeated analysis of the same assay image yields identical results, facilitating comparisons 400 

across research groups. 401 

## Limitations and future work 402 

Despite its overall robustness, several limitations were observed. Segmentation performance can degrade in 403 

cases where leaves overlap extensively or when background artifacts exhibit color characteristics similar to 404 

22/26 leaf tissue. Such errors may propagate to downstream lesion estimation, emphasizing the importance of 405 

standardized assay layout and imaging conditions. 406 

Additionally, lesion area estimation is formulated as a regression problem that predicts aggregate lesion 407 

area without explicitly delineating lesion boundaries. While this approach avoids the ambiguity associated 408 

with pixel-level lesion annotation, it does not provide spatial localization of lesions within individual leaves. 409 

Meanwhile, the occlusion map shown in Fig. 9 only indicates roughly which regions most influence the 410 

NASNet A prediction, rather than explicit lesion boundaries. Consequently, the current implementation is 411 

best suited for applications where total lesion burden is the primary metric of interest rather than detailed 412 

lesion morphology. 413 

Future work will focus on extending the pipeline to additional pathosystems and symptom types through 414 

retraining of the lesion estimation model on new datasets. Further improvements may also include enhanced 415 

handling of complex assay layouts and the incorporation of alternative lesion quantification strategies when 416 

spatial lesion information is required. 417 

# Conclusion 418 

In this study, we presented LIME, a fully automated pipeline for high-throughput quantification of leaf lesion 419 

severity from assay images. By integrating zero-shot leaf segmentation with a deep learning–based lesion area 420 

estimation model, LIME enables objective and reproducible measurement of plant disease severity across 421 

large-scale phenotyping experiments. 422 

Comprehensive evaluation on Arabidopsis thaliana leaves infected with Sclerotinia sclerotiorum 423 

demonstrated that the proposed approach achieves consistent prediction accuracy across lesion-size groups 424 

and outperforms color-based baselines. Model accuracy was comparable to observed intrarater variability in 425 

manual scoring, while reducing analysis time by an order of magnitude relative to human annotation. These 426 

results indicate that LIME provides a practical, scalable, and reliable solution for quantitative lesion analysis 427 

in standardized plant pathology assays. 428
