Abstract 

Despite advances in object detection, aerial imagery remains a challenging domain, as models often fail to generalize across variations in spatial resolu-tion, scene composition, and semantic label coverage. Differences in geographic context, sensor characteristics, and object distributions across datasets limit the capacity of conventional models to learn consistent and transferable rep-resentations. Shared methods trained on such data tend to impose a unified representation across fundamentally different domains, resulting in poor perfor-mance on region-specific content and less flexibility when dealing with novel object categories. To address this, we propose a novel modular learning framework that enables structured specialization in aerial detection. Our method intro-duces a hierarchical routing mechanism with two levels of modularity: a global expert assignment layer that uses latent geographic embeddings to route datasets to specialized processing modules, and a local scene decomposition mechanism that allocates image subregions to region-specific sub-modules. This allows our method to specialize across datasets and within complex scenes. Additionally, the framework contains a conditional expert module that uses external seman-tic information (e.g., category names or textual descriptions) to enable detection of novel object categories during inference, without the need for retraining or fine-tuning. By moving beyond monolithic representations, our method offers an adaptive framework for remote sensing object detection. Comprehensive evalu-ations on four datasets highlight improvements in multi-dataset generalization, regional specialization, and open-category detection.

# 1 Introduction 

Object detection in aerial or remote sensing imagery (RSI) refers to identifying objects of interest on the Earth surface [1–3]. This task is important for interpreting remote sensing data and supporting a range of earth observation applications, including envi-ronmental monitoring and disaster response systems [4, 5]. Recent advancements in deep learning, particularly with the rise of convolutional neural networks (CNNs), have significantly improved aerial object detection [1, 6, 7]. Despite these improvements, object detection in RSI continues to pose considerable challenges due to the substantial diversity in data sources and scene structures [8]. Unlike natural image benchmarks [9, 10], remote sensing datasets [11–14] are collected across different geographic regions and sensor platforms, each with its own characteristics in spatial resolution, seman-tic coverage, and object distribution. This heterogeneity introduces significant domain shifts, making it difficult for a unified model to capture generalizable representations across datasets [15]. A typical approach to address multi-domain object detection is training a shared model over concatenated datasets [16, 17]. While this strategy provides a simple solution, it often results in biased learning, where dominant datasets or frequently occurring object types overshadow less represented domains. Such shared architec-tures lack the capacity to adapt to domain-specific variations or to focus selectively on geographically or structurally distinct regions within a scene. To overcome these limitations, recent methods have explored multi-task learning [18, 19] and Mixture of Experts (MoE) [20–22] frameworks. Multi-task approaches introduce dataset-specific components to encourage partial specialization, while MoE models introduce expert modules and gating functions that dynamically route inputs, allowing selective pro-cessing based on content. However, these models often operate at the token level with soft attention, resulting in fragmented or overlapping expert usage. Their gating mech-anisms lack semantic structure, and expert usage is imbalanced. More importantly, as shown on Fig. 1 (left) they ignore the spatial context inherent to remote sensing imagery, where object appearance and meaning are often determined by region-specific 2visual patterns. Moreover, most models rely on fixed label spaces and cannot generalize to novel categories during inference [23–25]. These challenges require a detection framework that can move beyond uniform representations and instead adapt structurally to the diversity of remote sensing data. Specifically, the framework should support specialization across heterogeneous datasets, adapt to spatial variability within individual scenes, and flexibly extend to novel categories at inference time without requiring manual reconfiguration or retraining. To address these challenges, we propose Hierarchical Modular Routing Net-work (HMR-Net), that introduces structured, multi-level specialization into the object detection pipeline. Instead of relying on a single shared backbone, the model is com-posed of modular components that are selectively activated based on dataset identity and scene composition. As shown in Fig. 1 (right), at the dataset level, we introduce a global expert routing mechanism that learns latent geographic embeddings to repre-sent each dataset and uses them to assign inputs to domain-specialized expert modules. This enables the model to capture coarse domain characteristics such as those present in urban, rural, or coastal imagery without relying on manual dataset structuring. At the image level, we introduce a content-aware spatial parsing strategy that adaptively splits each image into region-specific subspaces, enabling targeted expert selection based on localized visual context. These region-level experts enable the model to capture fine-grained, spatially localized structures, supporting contextual adapta-tion within a single scene, such as roads in dense urban zones or vessels in coastal image. By integrating these two levels of modularity, our method achieves both inter-domain adaptability and intra-scene specialization within a unified design. To enhance its flexibility, we extend the method specialization capability by allowing it to con-ditionally align with external semantic cues. Specifically, we introduce a mechanism for latent alignment between visual representations and text inputs, enabling the model to respond to object categories not present during training. This supports open-category inference through prompt adaptation, without requiring explicit supervision or retraining. In doing so, the method moves beyond fixed label spaces and acquires the flexibility needed for real-world remote sensing tasks. Through this formulation, our method achieves a balance between specialization and generalization, capturing both domain-level variations across datasets and spa-tial complexity within scenes. Its ability to condition expert behaviour on external semantic information further enables inference on object categories not present during training, addressing a key limitation of existing closed-set detectors. Comprehensive experiments on diverse aerial imagery benchmarks demonstrate that our proposed approach consistently improves detection performance, particularly in cross-domain generalization, fine-grained spatial localization, and open-category recognition. These results highlight the potential of our modular learning as a principled and effec-tive direction for advancing object detection in complex and diverse remote sensing environments. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: 

• We propose HMR-Net for RSI object detection that introduces structured special-ization at both dataset and image-region levels. This method incorporates a global 3expert routing mechanism based on latent geographic embeddings and a local scene segmentation strategy for spatially adaptive expert selection. 

• We extend the modular framework with a conditional specialization mechanism, which allows expert behaviour to be guided by text inputs. 

• Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed approach significantly improves cross-domain generalization and region-level localization, with particular advantages in detecting previously unseen categories. These results highlight the scalability and label efficiency of our method for remote sensing applications. The rest of this manuscript is structured as follows: Section 2 discusses related studies. In Section 3, we introduce the details of HMR-Net. Section 4 presents the experimental results and ablation study. Finally, Section 5 concluding the remarks. 

# 2 Related Work 

## 2.1 Object Detection in Remote Sensing 

Object detection in remote sensing has been addressed through a range of deep learn-ing approaches adapted from general-purpose detection models [1, 26, 27], including two-stage detectors like Faster R-CNN [4, 28] and one-stage variants such as YOLO [29, 30] and RetinaNet [31, 32]. Moreover, attention [3, 33], transformer [34–36] and feature enhancement-based methods [37, 38] have also been extended to this domain. These methods have shown strong performance on individual benchmarks such as DOTA, xView, and DIOR [11–13], which vary widely in resolution, object density, and scene type. Despite these advances, most models are developed and evaluated on a per-dataset basis and are rarely designed to operate across multiple sources [1, 11, 39]. Cross-dataset generalization remains limited, as models tend to overfit to dataset-specific characteristics and struggle with variations in scene structure and label semantics. Several works have attempted to improve transferability by apply-ing domain adaptation techniques [4, 40, 41] or training unified models over merged datasets [16, 42]. However, these approaches often rely on shared backbones and global representations, which are insufficient to capture the spatial and semantic diversity inherent to remote sensing. This has motivated the need for detection frameworks that can explicitly model domain-specific variations, local scene structures, and evolving object semantics challenges that remain insufficiently addressed by current approaches. 

## 2.2 Multi-Dataset Object Detection 

Object detection has long been a foundational task in computer vision, with contin-ued efforts aimed at developing models that generalize across a wide range of object categories and visual environments. One of the key challenges in this area is the limited availability of large-scale, well-annotated datasets, as collecting and labeling high-quality data is both expensive and time-consuming [43]. To address this limita-tion, recent research has explored the use of multiple datasets in a unified training framework, aiming to use the visual diversity across sources to improve robustness and generalization in detection tasks [44–46]. Training object detectors using multiple 4datasets is a widely adopted strategy for improving generalization, especially where individual datasets lack sufficient diversity or scale. By combining datasets, models are exposed to a broader range of object categories, image conditions, and domain-specific variations. However, this approach introduces several challenges, including differences in dataset sizes, annotation coverage, label semantics, and scene structures. Early works focused on jointly training large-scale datasets such as ImageNet and OpenImages [47] to increase visual diversity and category coverage. However, inconsis-tencies in annotation density, label definitions, and domain characteristics limited their effectiveness. To address these limitations, adaptation mechanisms such as squeeze-and-excitation layers were proposed to adjust feature representations based on dataset specifics [48], but these methods relied on shared architectures and lacked mechanisms to address semantic misalignment across datasets. Recent methods have addressed annotation inconsistencies by designing loss functions capable of learning from par-tially labeled data and incorporating pseudo-labels to supplement missing annotations. To handle differences in label definitions across datasets, several approaches have introduced language-guided embeddings to construct shared label spaces [45, 46]. Other strategies use unified architectures with dataset-specific output layers and projection layers to reconcile heterogeneous label definitions during training [49]. Addi-tionally, mixture-of-experts models have been proposed to assign each dataset to a dedicated expert, enabling more structured learning under domain imbalance and dis-tribution shift [20, 43, 50]. While effective in capturing dataset-specific characteristics, these models typically rely on fixed expert assignment strategies, which limit flexibility when dataset boundaries are ambiguous or when distributions overlap significantly. In contrast, our proposed HMR-Net introduces a more adaptive and scalable solution by enabling expert specialization at both the dataset and region levels, guided by learned routing mechanisms based on domain and spatial diversity. 

## 2.3 Modular and Expert-Based Learning 

Recent work in modular and expert-based learning [51–53] has explored architec-tures designed to improve adaptability, scalability, and specialization across tasks or domains. Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models have been widely adopted in vision tasks for their ability to route inputs to a subset of specialized modules, allowing the net-work to adjust its capacity based on the input. Several MoE-based object detectors have used soft routing strategies to distribute computational resources across token-level experts, achieving improvements in model efficiency and representation diversity. In MoE, the router (gate) decides which expert is responsible for each token or sample. Several routing strategies have been proposed, such as Top-k token routing [54, 55], expert-driven token selection [49, 56], and refining expert selection [57, 58]. However, a primary challenge in these frameworks is imbalanced expert loads. Routers often overuse some experts while overlooking others, resulting in inefficient capacity use. To address this, [59] introduces a balancing loss that encourages equal expert use, and subsequent studies have further developed these concepts [60, 61]. Related work in calibrated expert learning offers further insight into expert specialization and bal-anced contribution. Despite their success, existing approaches have several limita-tions, particularly in complex domains such as remote sensing. Most models rely on probabilistic gating mechanisms that lead to partial expert activation, where multiple experts contribute weakly without developing distinct functional roles. This behaviour is evident in Fig. 2 (left), where assignments appear scattered and unstructured, with experts overlapping across all domains rather than forming clear specializations. Such diffuse activation undermines the modular structure and limits interpretability since no expert consistently aligns with a specific task or dataset. Moreover, MoE models typically operate at the token or patch level, without incorporating higher-level con-textual details such as domain identity, scene composition, or geographic patterns. As a result, these models struggle to develop meaningful specialization across diverse data sources. Current architectures also rely on flat gating, which is insufficient for cap-turing both broad domain-level variation and detailed spatial structure within scenes. These limitations highlight the need for a more structured, context-aware learning method capable of addressing both cross-domain variation and scene-level complexity.

Unlike earlier Mixture of Experts (MoE) [53] approaches, which depend only on token-level visual features, our approach explicitly incorporates dataset-specific attributes, leading to interpretable and domain-aware specialization. Aerial images are complex, often containing multiple spatially distinct regions that correspond to different semantic categories or structural patterns. A single scene might contain areas of dense urban structures, vegetation, and water bodies, each demand-ing distinct modeling strategies. Global experts alone are insufficient to handle this complexity, as they do not explicitly model localized spatial variations within images. To address this challenge, we introduce the local region routing mechanism as the second stage in our hierarchical modular architecture. This mechanism adaptively seg-ments the global expert feature maps into spatial routing units (RUs) and routes each RU independently to an appropriate local expert module. In this process, a dynamic partitioning strategy is used that adaptively determines the number of regions per image according to scene complexity, enabling an optimized balance between regional specificity and feature representation.

On average, the CEM processes at most 16 alignments per image, incurring less than 2% additional inference time (see Fig. 14). The aligned outputs are processed by two lightweight detection heads: a classification head that supports both fixed-category classification and open-category detection, and a bounding box regression head for localization. The classification head switches between standard and prompt-guided modes depending on the inference context. 13 3.5 Training

# 4 Experiments 

This section presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed HMR-Net. We eval-uate its performance across a diverse set of remote sensing benchmarks, focusing on three core capabilities: generalization across domains, specialization within complex scenes, and recognition of novel object categories. We conduct comparisons against existing state-of-the-art object detection models to highlight the benefits of modular specialization and structured routing. The experimental setup, evaluation protocols, and implementation details are described below. 

## 4.1 Experimental Setup 

Datasets. We evaluate our model using four aerial object detection benchmarks: DIOR [11] consists of 23,463 images across 20 object categories, including vehicles, buildings, aircraft, ships, and storage tanks. It covers multiple scenes, such as urban, rural, and industrial, captured at varying scales and resolutions. DOTA-v1.0 [12] contains high-resolution imagery showing complex spatial configura-tions and detailed scene compositions. It has 2,806 images of 15 object classes. xView [13] is a large-scale dataset designed for detecting small and rare objects in overhead satellite imagery. It contains approximately 1 million labeled instances across 60 object categories, featuring both common classes and less frequent classes, making it valuable for testing the detection of sparsely represented objects. NWPU VHR-10 [73] provides 800 high-resolution images of 10 object categories. 

Implementation Details. For all main experiments (to ensure fair comparison to existing RS baselines), we use HMR-Net with a Faster R-CNN R50-FPN head [74] (ImageNet-pretrained). HMR-Net itself is detector-agnostic : the fused feature map oij

produced by hierarchical routing is passed to the detection head. In the proposal-based setting, an RPN generates RoIs from oij and standard classification/box heads are applied. In a DETR [75] setting, oij serves as the encoder input with positional encodings and detections are decoded from object queries. Routing and expert selec-tion occur before the head, therefore the same oij is produced regardless of whether the head is proposal-based or query-based. Given the semantic overlap across datasets, a strict one-to-one mapping between datasets and experts limits flexibility. Instead, we use six global experts, allowing the model to form semantic clusters across datasets. This allows the routing function to specialize experts based on visual content rather than dataset origin, improving generalization across shared object categories and scene types. All expert modules (global, local, and conditional) are implemented as lightweight sub-networks composed of three convolutional layers followed by ReLU activations and batch normalization. We set the number of global experts to EG = 6 in all main experiments. This is based 15 Fig. 5 Visualization detection results of our model on sample images from different datasets. 

on an ablation study (see Fig. 13), where increasing EG improves both performance and routing balance up to six experts. For the conditional expert, additional linear projection layers are included to align visual embeddings with CLIP semantic space. These modules are trained from scratch and process backbone feature maps through the hierarchical routing pipeline before being passed to detection heads. Training is conducted using stochastic gradi-ent descent with a momentum of 0 .9, an initial learning rate of 0 .001, and a weight decay of 1 e − 4. The model is trained for 200 epochs and the learning rate is decayed by a factor of 0 .1 at epochs 120 and 160. θR is initialized to 1 .0. We use a batch size 8 across four RTX-3090 GPUs for training and use one GPU for testing. Dur-ing training, data augmentations, including random horizontal flipping, scaling, and random rotation, are applied. All datasets are preprocessed to a fixed input size of 800 ×800. For DOTA and xView, we adopt a tiling strategy with non-overlapping crops, ensuring that object annotations are preserved within each tile. For NWPU and DIOR, we resized or padded the images to the target resolution. For consistency across all datasets, we convert all annotations to horizontal bounding boxes (HBB). While DOTA and xView contain inherently rotated objects, this simplification enables a unified detection protocol and fair comparison across datasets. At inference time, Non-Maximum Suppression is used with an IoU threshold of 0 .5. The hyperparame-ters associated with routing regularization and prompt alignment losses are kept fixed across all experiments.

4.3.1 Hierarchical Modular Routing 

To assess the contribution of each routing stage, we compare three configurations: global-only, local-only, and the full model. In the global-only setting, images are assigned to a single expert based on dataset-specific embeddings, and detection is per-formed using the expert dedicated head over shared region features. In the local-only setting, global routing is disabled, and tokens are directly assigned to local experts via a content-aware gating function, with outputs aggregated for detection. The full model combines both stages, enabling hierarchical routing from domain-level assignment to token-level specialization. As shown in Table 10 and Fig. 9, both routing components contribute positively to overall detection performance. The global routing achieves reasonable results by using dataset-level priors. However, it underperforms on smaller and medium-sized objects, indicating limited capacity to adapt to fine-grained variations within an image. The local region routing improves performance across all object scales by enabling content-driven expert specialization, leading to stronger AP S and AP M scores, particularly on complex datasets (e.g. xView). Still, without global domain features, the model is prone to suboptimal expert usage in cases of strong dataset-level shifts. The full HMR-Net consistently outperforms both variants across all metrics and datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of combining domain-aware routing with fine-grained token-level specialization. Notably, it achieves 81 .24 AP 50 on DOTA and 49 .57 on xView, with consistent gains across all object sizes. These results highlight that hierarchical routing enables the model to maintain global consistency while capturing local structural and semantic diversity. # 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce Hierarchical Modular Routing Network (HMR-Net), a modular detection framework that enables structured specialization across datasets and within complex aerial scenes. By introducing global expert routing, local region segmentation, and a conditional expert module for open-category detection, HMR-Net achieves strong adaptability and transferability. Extensive experiments show that our model outperforms existing methods in multi-domain generalization, region-level spe-cialization, and zero-shot detection. These results highlight HMR-Net as an effective and scalable solution for remote sensing object detection. While HMR-Net achieves strong specialization with a moderate number of experts, scaling to larger expert pools may lead to redundancy. An important extension will be to design adaptive expert pruning, so that the architecture remains efficient while preserving functional diversity. 28 Data Availability
