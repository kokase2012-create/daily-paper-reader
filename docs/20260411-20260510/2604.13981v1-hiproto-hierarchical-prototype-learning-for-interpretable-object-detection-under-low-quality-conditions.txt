# HiProto: Hierarchical Prototype Learning for Interpretable Object Detection Under Low-quality Conditions 

Jianlin Xiang, Linhui Dai ∗, Xue Yang, Chaolei Yang, and Yanshan Li 

Abstract —Interpretability is essential for deploying object detection systems in critical applications, especially under low-quality imaging conditions that degrade visual information and increase prediction uncertainty. Existing methods either enhance image quality or design complex architectures, but often lack interpretability and fail to improve semantic discrimination. In contrast, prototype learning enables interpretable modeling by associating features with class-centered semantics, which can pro-vide more stable and interpretable representations under degra-dation. Motivated by this, we propose HiProto, a new paradigm for interpretable object detection based on hierarchical prototype learning. By constructing structured prototype representations across multiple feature levels, HiProto effectively models class-specific semantics, thereby enhancing both semantic discrimina-tion and interpretability. Building upon prototype modeling, we first propose a Region-to-Prototype Contrastive Loss (RPC-Loss) to enhance the semantic focus of prototypes on target regions. Then, we propose a Prototype Regularization Loss (PR-Loss) to improve the distinctiveness among class prototypes. Finally, we propose a Scale-aware Pseudo Label Generation Strategy (SPLGS) to suppress mismatched supervision for RPC-Loss, thereby preserving the robustness of low-level prototype repre-sentations. Experiments on ExDark, RTTS, and VOC2012-FOG demonstrate that HiProto achieves competitive results while offer-ing clear interpretability through prototype responses, without re-lying on image enhancement or complex architectures. Our code will be available at https://github.com/xjlDestiny/HiProto.git. 

Index Terms —Object detection, prototype learning, hierarchi-cal feature modeling, low-quality imaging, interpretability.

A. Low-quality Object Detection Methods 

To tackle degraded visual conditions such as low light and haze, early works typically apply image enhancement algorithms [16]–[21] as a preprocessing step. However, these methods are often detached from detection objectives, risking feature inconsistency and information redundancy. Recent advances favor end-to-end designs that couple im-age restoration with detection. For instance, HLA-Face [22] and MAET [23] leverage multi-task supervision or disentan-gled learning to bridge the low- and high-quality domains. DSNet [24] and IA-YOLO [25] incorporate dedicated en-hancement modules to mitigate representation degradation. FeatEnHancer [26] and T2 [27] exploit illumination-aware cues to guide low-light detection. In addition, YOLA [28] avoids enhancement by learning illumination-invariant fea-tures. While effective, these methods often increase model com-plexity and lack interpretability. In contrast, we approach the problem from a feature modeling perspective by introducing a structured prototype-based detection architecture that improves semantic clarity without relying on image enhancement or complex architectural designs. 

B. Prototype Learning in Visual Tasks 

Prototype learning has attracted increasing attention in vi-sual tasks due to its interpretable feature matching, which enables effective modeling of class-level semantics. In image classification, ProtoPNet [10] and its variants [29]– [31] construct interpretable prototype representations to guide prediction through case-based reasoning, offering strong visual interpretability and semantic alignment. In semantic segmen-tation, prototype learning is mainly used in weakly supervised scenes, where SIPE [32], CPAL [33], and MuP-VSS [34] intro-duce image-specific, context-aware, or multi-label prototypes to improve region completeness, mitigate classification bias, and enhance semantic alignment. In object detection, prototype learning is mainly applied in few-shot scenes, where ICPE [35] integrates query-aware mechanisms with dynamic aggregation to generate adaptive prototypes, and IPNet [36] constructs instance-level prototype alignment and foreground-background attention transfer modules to significantly reduce domain gaps. While prototype learning shows promise in vision tasks, its potential for multi-scale semantic modeling remains underex-plored, especially under low-quality conditions where robust representations are crucial. To bridge this gap, we propose a hi-erarchical prototype learning architecture specifically designed for object detection under low-quality conditions, aiming to enhance both semantic discrimination and interpretability. B. Hierarchical Prototype Modeling 

As a classic multi-scale feature extraction method, the FPN constructs multi-resolution feature maps to enhance the ability of the model to perceive objects of various sizes. Actually, each spatial location in a feature map corresponds to a region within a certain receptive field of the original image, reflecting the semantic response and cues of object presence at that scale. Therefore, multi-scale feature maps not only carry spatial information but also imply rich semantic hierarchical struc-tures, providing a solid foundation for hierarchical prototype modeling. Based on this, we introduce a hierarchical prototype modeling mechanism into the multi-scale feature extraction, enabling each layer to focus on learning prototype repre-sentations of object semantics within its corresponding scale range. This approach enhances the discriminative capability and robustness of the model when detecting multi-scale objects under low-quality imaging conditions. C. Hierarchical Prototype Learning 

To fully exploit the advantages of hierarchical prototype modeling, we propose a prototype-driven feature optimization method composed of two modules: RPC-Loss and PR-Loss. The former enhances structural consistency between local features and semantic prototypes, while the latter improves the distinctiveness among class prototypes. In addition, to generate reliable pseudo label maps for RPC-Loss, we propose the SPLGS, which restricts supervision at each feature layer to appropriately sized objects to avoid label noise. In the following, the implementation details of the two modules are introduced using the l-th feature layer as an example.

A. Evaluation Metric 

To comprehensively evaluate our proposed model, we con-sider both detection performance and interpretability aspects. Specifically, we report the mean Average Precision at IoU threshold 0.5 (mAP@0.5) to assess detection accuracy, and further introduce three metrics to quantify interpretability. The Discriminability Score measures how well the activation en-ergy of each class concentrates within the ground-truth regions by aggregating saliency maps across all feature levels. The AUC ft further evaluates the separability between foreground and background activations, providing a threshold-independent measure of localization quality. Higher Discriminability Score and AUC ft values indicate that the learned prototypes respond more selectively and discriminatively to their corresponding objects. The Sparsity Score quantifies the degree of sparsity among prototype vectors of different classes within each hierarchy. A higher Sparsity Score indicates lower redundancy and stronger class-wise separation among the learned represen-tations. 

B. Datasets 

To evaluate the performance of the proposed method, we conduct model training and testing on three benchmark datasets: Exclusively Dark (ExDark) [55], Real-world Task-Driven Testing Set (RTTS) [56], and VOC2012-FOG. 

ExDark is a low-light object detection dataset consisting of 7,363 real-world images across twelve categories. We randomly split the dataset into 5,896 training and 1,467 testing images. 

RTTS is a real-world hazy dataset from the RESIDE bench-mark, containing 4,322 images for five categories: bicycle, bus, car, motorbike, and person. We use 3,889 images for training and 433 for testing. 

VOC2012-FOG is a synthetic hazy dataset derived from Pascal VOC2012 [57], generated using the atmospheric scat-tering model: 

I(x) = J(x) · e−βd (x) + A(1 − e−βd (x)), (15) where J(x) is the clear image, A denotes the global atmo-spheric light, β is the scattering coefficient, and d(x) is defined as: 

d(x) = −0.04 · ρ + pmax( row , col ). (16) In our implementation, we set A = 0 .5 and β = 0 .1 to sim-ulate moderate haze conditions. Hazy images are synthesized offline to reduce training overhead while preserving original annotations. The dataset contains a total of 11,540 images, with 9,232 used for training and 2,308 for testing.

F. Ablation Study 

To systematically analyze the contributions of different design choices in the proposed framework, we conduct com-prehensive ablation studies on three challenging datasets, including ExDark, RTTS, and VOC2012-FOG. 

1) Effects of Different Modules: Table V presents the results of different module combinations. When only RPC-Loss is applied, the performance improvement is limited because no explicit mechanism is imposed to reduce prototype redundancy and enhance class-wise prototype distinctiveness. In contrast, employing PR-Loss alone leads to notable performance gains across most datasets, highlighting its effectiveness in promot-ing more discriminative and independent prototype represen-tations. The best performance is achieved when all modules are jointly integrated, confirming their strong complementarity and validating the overall design of the proposed method. 

2) Effects of Different Feature Layers: We further evaluate the contribution of individual feature layers by restricting the model to use prototypes from a single layer. The results, reported in Table VI, show that different prototype layers specialize in capturing objects at different scales: the 8 × 8

layer performs best for small objects, the 16 ×16 layer is most effective for medium-sized objects, and the 32 ×32 layer excels at large-object detection. This observation confirms that the proposed hierarchical prototype modeling effectively captures scale-specific semantics and justifies the multi-level design of HiProto. 

3) Effects of Hyperparameter Settings: We investigate the influence of different hyperparameter configurations 

(τ1, τ 2, τ 3) from both efficiency and accuracy perspectives. JOURNAL OF L ATEX CLASS FILES, VOL. 14, NO. 8, AUGUST 2021 11 

Specifically, we analyze how prototype allocation across hi-erarchical levels affects computational cost, inference speed, and detection performance. Table VII reports the efficiency-related results under differ-ent settings. Results show that as (τ1, τ 2) increase, the model contains more parameters and requires higher computational cost, which leads to a gradual drop in FPS. Extremely large settings also reduce efficiency, indicating that a balanced hi-erarchical distribution provides the optimal trade-off between computational overhead and representation capacity. Table VIII presents the accuracy analysis. The results show that the best performance is achieved when τ1 = 4 , τ2 = 8 ,and τ3 = H/s 3, indicating the importance of proper scale separation. Here, H denotes the input image height and s3 is the stride of the final feature map. 

4) Effectiveness of Prototype Regularization Loss: To eval-uate the impact of different constraint strategies, we compare three implementations of PR-Loss using cosine loss, POP, and the proposed SVD-based constraint. The quantitative results are reported in Table IX. Across all datasets, the SVD-based implementation consistently achieves the best mAP, indicating that enforcing prototype independence with a global constraint is more effective than relying on pairwise penalties. Among the pairwise methods, POP outperforms the cosine-based variant, suggesting that soft inner-product constraints provide more stable and effective regularization. Nevertheless, both pairwise strategies yield smaller and less consistent gains compared to the proposed approach. Overall, these results demonstrate that the SVD-based PR-Loss offers a more robust and effective way to regularize prototypes, leading to improved detection performance under different visibility degradation scenarios. V. C ONCLUSION 

In this paper, we propose HiProto, a new paradigm for interpretable object detection under low-quality imaging con-ditions. Without relying on any image enhancement techniques or complex architectural designs, HiProto achieves effec-tive multi-scale semantic modeling and feature discrimination through structured hierarchical prototype learning. Specif-ically, we propose a prototype-driven feature optimization method that incorporates both Region-to-Prototype Contrastive Loss (RPC-Loss) and Prototype Regularization Loss (PR-Loss), where the former strengthens the semantic focus of pro-totypes on target regions, and the latter enhances the distinc-tiveness between class prototypes. Additionally, we propose a Scale-aware Pseudo Label Generation Strategy (SPLGS) that guides prototypes at different feature layers to focus on scale-appropriate objects, thereby reducing supervision noise.
